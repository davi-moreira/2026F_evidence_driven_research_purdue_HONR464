## Purpose

The declare → diagnose → redesign loop that you practiced on other people's
studies now closes on your own, with real audience data in hand. Each piece of
feedback implicates a specific part of your design — a MIDA letter — and points
to a specific fix. This session turns the coded tally (M17) into a triaged
redesign: what you change NOW, in the two weeks left, and what you'd change NEXT,
in the study you'd run again.

## The feedback → MIDA mapping method

Every substantive question lands on one of the four MIDA letters. Name the letter
BEFORE proposing a fix — the letter tells you what KIND of fix is even possible.

- **M — Model:** the world, mechanism, and assumptions (your DAG).
- **I — Inquiry:** the exact quantity you seek (your estimand, your "so what").
- **D — Data strategy:** who is sampled, how treatment/measurement happen, the frame.
- **A — Answer strategy:** how the data become the number (your estimator).

### Three worked examples

1. **A generalization question → D (Data strategy).**
   Visitor: *"Does this hold beyond the people in your sample?"* This implicates your
   sampling — the fix lives in D: a broader frame, or an honest re-statement of
   the population the claim reaches. Diagnosed benefit: the claim's boundary now
   matches its evidence.

2. **A mechanism question → M (Model).**
   Visitor: *"WHY would X move Y — what's the pathway?"* This implicates your model
   of the world — the fix lives in M: an added node, a stated mechanism, or a
   named alternative path in the DAG. Diagnosed benefit: the world your claim
   assumes is now explicit and challengeable.

3. **A so-what question → I (Inquiry).**
   Visitor: *"Suppose you're right — what quantity actually matters here?"* This
   implicates the inquiry — the fix lives in I: sharpening the estimand to the
   quantity a decision-maker needs. Diagnosed benefit: the answer now targets a
   question worth answering.

## NOW-vs-NEXT triage rules

Mark each redesign **NOW** or **NEXT**:

- **NOW** if it is achievable in the two weeks before the dossier AND it changes
  what you can honestly claim (a reframe, a sensitivity check, a boundary
  correction, a clearer figure).
- **NEXT** if it requires data, time, or resources you don't have (a new sample,
  an experiment, a semester you don't get). NEXT items are real science — they
  belong in the dossier's "if I ran it again" section, not this week's task list.
- The test for NOW: *can I do it before Dec 11, and does it move a claim?* If
  either answer is no, it's NEXT.

## The redesign-declaration format

For each of your top three feedback items:

> **Feedback item:** *(the question, quoted)*
> **Implicated letter:** *(M / I / D / A)*
> **Redesign:** *(the specific change)*
> **Diagnosed benefit:** *(the diagnosand it improves — sharper claim, honest
> boundary, probed assumption, tighter estimand)*
> **NOW or NEXT:** *(and, if NOW, the dossier task it becomes)*

A redesign with no letter and no diagnosand is a lament ("more data, more time"),
not a redesign. The mapping is what makes it scientific.

## Procedure (50 minutes)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 | Tally-pattern recap (two headlines). |
| 5–12 | Mini-lesson: mapping feedback onto MIDA — the three worked examples above. |
| 12–30 | **Redesign studio:** map your top-3 items to letters, propose a redesign for each, mark NOW/NEXT; rotating consults. |
| 30–42 | **Redesign declarations:** 2 minutes each — your most important redesign, its letter, its diagnosed benefit. |
| 42–48 | Lock the NOW items into your dossier task list. |
| 48–50 | Exit ticket (your #1 redesign, its letter, NOW or NEXT); M18 due tonight. |

## Roles

- **You:** name the letter before the fix; be honest about NOW vs NEXT.
- **Consult partner / instructor:** challenge any mapping that skipped the letter or any NEXT smuggled in as NOW.

### Session flow (for the record)

Professor Moreira demos the three mappings, runs rotating consults during the
studio, times the 2-minute declarations, and stages the dossier task-list
template. Each person declares once; NOW items become each dossier's work plan.

## Common failure modes

1. **The wish-list.** "More data, more time" implicates no letter and diagnoses no benefit. Map it or drop it.
2. **Skipping the letter.** Proposing a fix before naming M/I/D/A produces the wrong kind of fix.
3. **Everything is NOW.** Overloading two weeks guarantees nothing ships. Be ruthless: most redesigns are NEXT.
4. **Regret dressed as redesign.** "I wish I'd picked a better topic" is not a MIDA move. Extract the letter-level change or file it under NEXT.
5. **Ignoring communication-only feedback.** If M17 tagged it communication, the fix is the poster/brief, not the design — route it accordingly.
