# Presentation Plan Protocol — M32 (Mon Nov 9)

*How to build and rehearse the two spoken versions of your poster pitch.
Governs [milestone_13 — Presentation Plan](../../_research_project/2026Fall/milestone_13_presentation_plan.md).
Scripted in class M32; both scripts + walk map due Wed Nov 11.*

## Purpose

A visitor stops at your poster on Nov 17. Your first 60 seconds decide whether
they stay. You prepare two spoken versions — a 1-minute hook and a 3-minute walk
— that say exactly what the poster says, compressed but never inflated. The
cardinal rule: the mouth may compress the evidence, it may never upgrade the
claim.

## Materials

- Your final poster (content internalized, headline claim sayable cold).
- Your inquiry declaration §4–5 (the boundary you must not cross aloud).
- Your uncertainty line and the limitation you'll speak (feeds M14).
- A partner and a timer for the delivery rounds.

## The two architectures

**1-minute hook** — the job is to earn the next two minutes.

```
1. HOOK          one sentence a stranger outside your field cares about
2. CLAIM         your headline, spoken inside its boundary
3. BOUNDARY      one sentence: what this does NOT show ("this is an
                 association / a sample / a forecast, so it cannot say…")
4. INVITATION    "the surprising part is on the left — want the walk?"
```

**3-minute walk** — the hook, plus the evidence and the honest bound.

```
1. HOOK + CLAIM        (as above, tightened)
2. EVIDENCE BLOCK 1    strongest — what it shows, in one spoken sentence
3. EVIDENCE BLOCK 2    the support
4. EVIDENCE BLOCK 3    the weakest kept, honestly labeled
5. ULN MOMENT          uncertainty → limitation → next step (the M14 move)
6. INVITATION          hand the question back: "what would you probe first?"
```

The 3-minute version **adds** blocks and the ULN moment to the 1-minute spine —
it does not restate a longer, looser claim. Every evidence sentence is a
compression of a poster block, checked against it word for word.

## The compression-never-inflation rule

Compression drops detail; inflation adds certainty. Saying "in our sample, most
respondents…" as "people generally…" is inflation — the excited-mouth upgrade
from "is associated with" to "causes" is the classic one. Your boundary sentence
is the antidote: rehearse it until it is automatic, so the claim can never
outrun it live.

## Procedure (50 minutes)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 | Poster-phase debrief + what Nov 17 actually looks like (logistics). |
| 5–13 | Mini-lesson: the two architectures, demonstrated live — once well, once inflated. You spot the exact word that upgraded the claim. |
| 13–28 | **Scripting studio:** write your 1-minute hook on the template; mark your boundary sentence. |
| 28–42 | **Delivery round:** deliver the 1-minute to a partner **twice** (swap roles between). The partner flags every spoken upgrade. |
| 42–48 | Revise the script from the flags. |
| 48–50 | Exit ticket (your boundary sentence, exactly as you'll say it). |

Homework: draft the 3-minute walk; the plan (both scripts + poster-walk map) is
due Wed Nov 11.

## Roles

- **Presenter:** delivers cold, from the script, twice — the first attempt is officially a draft.
- **Partner (upgrade-flagger):** listen only for claims that outrun the poster; raise a hand the instant a word inflates, and name the word.

### Session flow (for the record)

Professor Moreira demonstrates the well/inflated pair, times the twice-through
delivery rounds, and normalizes stage fright by making everyone's first attempt a
draft. Each presenter delivers twice and flags for one partner.

## Quality checklist (before submitting M13)

- ☐ 1-minute script has all four moves; the boundary sentence is marked.
- ☐ 3-minute walk adds evidence blocks + a ULN moment to the same spine.
- ☐ Every spoken claim matches the poster's written boundary — no upgrade.
- ☐ A poster-walk map shows the physical path your hand/eye takes at the board.
- ☐ Delivered aloud at least twice; partner flags addressed.

## Common failure modes

1. **The excited-mouth upgrade.** "Associated with" becomes "causes" when the adrenaline hits. Drill the boundary sentence until it's reflex.
2. **Inflating instead of compressing.** Dropping the frame word ("in our sample") is not shortening — it's overclaiming. Keep the qualifier, cut the aside.
3. **Two scripts, one claim-size.** If the 3-minute version says something bigger than the 1-minute, the extra time inflated the claim. Add evidence, not certainty.
4. **Reading, not delivering.** A script read verbatim loses the visitor. Rehearse to the point of eye contact.
5. **No boundary in the hook.** A 1-minute pitch with no "what this can't show" invites the overclaim the walk can't take back.
