## Purpose

"Here is what this study cannot show" makes an expert MORE believable, not less —
because it proves you know where your evidence stops. This session trains the
ULN move so your limitation lands as expertise, never as apology and never
hidden. Findings are never communicated as certainties (CLAUDE.md Uncertainty
rule); the ULN is how you honor that out loud.

## Materials

- Your findings and your uncertainty line (from the poster).
- The one limitation that most threatens your claim (from inquiry declaration §7).
- A partner and the strength-not-apology scale (below).

## The ULN move (uncertainty → limitation → next step)

Three sentences, in order, spine straight:

1. **Uncertainty** — how sure, and within what bound.
   *"The estimate is X, give or take about Y."*
2. **Limitation** — the specific thing the design cannot establish, and why.
   *"Because this is [an association / a convenience frame / one semester of data], it cannot show Z."*
3. **Next step** — the design that WOULD settle it.
   *"The study that could is W — here's what it would take."*

The next-step sentence is what converts a confession into a contribution. It
says: I know exactly what I'd do next, which is the mark of someone in command of
their evidence.

## The strength-not-apology scale (1–5)

Partners score each ULN delivery on this anchored scale:

| Score | What it sounds like |
|---|---|
| **1** | Apology spiral: *"this is probably all wrong, sorry, don't trust it."* Undermines a real finding. |
| **2** | Hedge-heavy: so many qualifiers the finding disappears. Uncertainty misstated as worthlessness. |
| **3** | Flat: limitation stated neutrally, no spine, no next step. Honest but inert. |
| **4** | Informative: limitation as information, with a next step. Credible. |
| **5** | Expertise: precise bound + specific limitation + the design that resolves it, delivered with a straight spine. The visitor trusts you MORE. |

Aim for 4–5. A 1 or 2 misstates the evidence just as badly as hiding the
limitation would — both are defects.

## The apology-spiral antidote

The spiral starts with a posture, not a sentence: shrinking. The antidote is
physical and verbal — plant your feet, drop "sorry" and "just" and "I know this
is bad," and end on the next step, not the flaw. You are reporting a boundary,
not confessing a crime.

## Sentence stems (use these, out loud)

- "Within the frame I can reach, the pattern is ___; beyond it, I can't say."
- "This is an association, so it cannot establish ___ — the design that could is ___."
- "The number moves by about ___ under my sensitivity check, which is why I claim ___ and not more."
- "What this can't show is ___. What it does show is ___. Here's how you'd find out the rest."
- (For a null:) "I did not find ___, and that absence is itself informative because ___."

## Procedure (50 minutes)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 | 1-minute pitch pulse (one volunteer, warm notes). |
| 5–12 | Mini-lesson: the ULN move on three findings, including a null. |
| 12–24 | **Writing studio:** draft your ULN paragraph (the M14 core). |
| 24–40 | **ULN rounds:** deliver to two successive partners; partners score on the 1–5 scale. |
| 40–47 | Whole-group: the best ULN heard, re-delivered for the room. |
| 47–50 | Exit ticket (your most important limitation in ULN form, ≤ 30 words); M13 due tonight. |

## Roles

- **Presenter:** delivers the three ULN sentences with a straight spine, ends on the next step.
- **Partner (scorer):** score 1–5, then name the ONE move that would raise the score a point.

### Session flow (for the record)

Professor Moreira demos three ULNs (one a null finding), times the two-partner
rounds, and if deliveries collapse into apology, pauses to re-demo the spine
posture with the stems on screen. Each presenter delivers twice and scores twice.

## Common failure modes

1. **The apology spiral.** "Sorry, this is probably wrong" throws away a real finding. Plant your feet, drop "sorry", end on the next step.
2. **Drowning in hedges.** Ten qualifiers hide the finding as surely as zero. State the bound once, clearly.
3. **Limitation with no next step.** A flaw named and abandoned scores a 3. The next-step sentence is what earns the 5.
4. **Inflating to avoid the limitation.** Overclaiming to dodge the caveat is the opposite failure and a worse one.
5. **A different limitation than the poster's.** The spoken bound must match the written one — pick the limitation that actually threatens your claim.
