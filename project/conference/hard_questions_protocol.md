## Purpose

The hardest fair question a stranger asks on Nov 17 should be one you already
wrote down and answered in November. This session builds your five-question bank
(one per recurring type) and drills it under fire, so that on Expo day you are
never surprised into bluffing — the one move that can destroy a presenter's
credibility entirely.

## Materials

- Your poster and its claim boundary.
- Your ULN paragraph (from M33) — several answers route back to it.
- Your AI-use ledger (you will be asked how you verified AI output).
- A blank bank sheet (five rows, one per type).

## The five question types (with an example each)

| Type | What it probes | Example a stranger might ask |
|---|---|---|
| **Method** | Whether your compass position can bear the claim | "Why is a description enough here — didn't you need a comparison group?" |
| **Alternative explanation** | A rival cause of your pattern | "Couldn't selection into your sample produce this on its own?" |
| **So-what** | Why the finding matters | "Suppose you're right — what changes because of it?" |
| **Generalization** | How far the claim travels | "Does this hold beyond the people in your sample?" |
| **AI-use** | The integrity of your process | "You used an AI tool to find sources — how did you confirm they exist?" |

Write ONE hard, fair version of each for your own poster, plus a two-line answer
sketch. The AI-use answer must point at your ledger and your verification step —
Ask → Verify → Document, performed.

## The honest-don't-know formula

When you genuinely don't know, say so — with substance:

> **"I don't know — but here is what I DO know that bears on it, and here is how
> one would find out."**

Three parts: the honest admission, the adjacent thing you can stand on, and the
method that would resolve it. This is a strong answer, not a weak one. Bluffing
an answer you don't have is the forbidden move; a substantive "I don't know" is
the credible one.

## Defend vs concede (the judgment)

- **Defend** when the ledger supports you: point at the row — the claim, its
  evidence, its verification. Rigor, not volume.
- **Concede** when the questioner is right: "That's a fair hit — it moves my
  claim from ___ to ___." A graceful concession raises your credibility; a
  dug-in defense of an indefensible point sinks it.
- The test: does my claim ledger back this claim? If yes, defend. If no, concede
  and state the smaller claim that survives.

## Hot-seat drill procedure

1. Each presenter takes the seat for **3 minutes** of rapid questions.
2. Questions come from the class **and** from Professor Moreira's surprise deck
   (questions your own bank didn't predict).
3. Answer in one or two sentences — this is reflex training, not an essay.
4. Professor Moreira models **one save** during the session: how to convert a
   stumble into an honest, grounded answer.
5. If the seat runs hot emotionally, Professor Moreira becomes questioner-of-record
   and slows the cadence — the drill's purpose is safety through practice.

## Procedure (50 minutes)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 | ULN redux (one volunteer, cold). |
| 5–12 | Mini-lesson: the five types with a real example each + the honest-don't-know formula. |
| 12–24 | **Bank-building studio:** write your five questions (one per type) with answer sketches. |
| 24–44 | **Hot-seat rounds:** 3 minutes per presenter (bank + surprise deck); instructor models one save. |
| 44–48 | Bank revision from what the hot seat exposed. |
| 48–50 | Exit ticket (the question I most fear + my first sentence in reply); M11 presentation package due tonight. |

## Roles

- **Presenter (in the seat):** answer fast, defend from the ledger or concede cleanly, use the don't-know formula when true.
- **Questioners (the class):** every non-presenter asks at least one question, aiming across the five types.

### Session flow (for the record)

Professor Moreira prepares a surprise-question deck per project, times the 3-minute
seats, models one save, and slows the room if a seat runs hot. Each presenter
sits once; each questioner asks across multiple seats.

## Quality checklist (before submitting M11)

- ☐ Five questions, one per type, each hard and fair to your own poster.
- ☐ A two-line answer sketch for each; the AI-use answer cites your ledger.
- ☐ At least one honest-don't-know answer prepared where you truly don't know.
- ☐ For each answer: marked defend or concede, consistent with your claim ledger.
- ☐ Bank revised from the hot-seat surprises.

## Common failure modes

1. **Bluffing.** Inventing an answer under pressure is the one unrecoverable move. Use the don't-know formula instead.
2. **Defending the indefensible.** If the ledger doesn't back it, concede and state the smaller true claim.
3. **Softball bank.** Easy questions you can already answer waste the drill. Write the ones you fear.
4. **Blaming the AI tool.** "The AI gave me that" is not a defense — you verified, or you didn't. Own the process.
5. **Essay answers.** The Expo floor rewards two crisp sentences, not a lecture. Drill for brevity.
