## Purpose

Strangers' questions reveal what friends' never could — where your project is
genuinely unclear or genuinely contested. But feedback is only useful if you
treat it like data: coded, counted, and read for its pattern. Applause is not
evidence your claims are true; a question asked three times is evidence of
something real. This session extracts that signal.

## Materials

- Every question and reaction you captured at the Expo (see logging method below).
- The nb12 tally cells (Colab).
- Professor Moreira's own observation notes from your poster (returned today).
- A partner for the tally swap.

## Capturing questions AT the Expo (do this Tuesday, while presenting)

You cannot code what you didn't capture. Log in the moment, lightly:

- Keep a phone note or an index card in your pocket. After each visitor leaves,
  jot **one line**: the question's gist + a one-word type tag.
- Star anything you heard **more than once** — recurrence is the signal you most
  want.
- Note the questions your bank did NOT predict — those are the richest.
- Don't transcribe mid-conversation; capture in the 20 seconds after each
  visitor moves on.

## The coding scheme

Code every logged question on three dimensions:

| Dimension | Values |
|---|---|
| **Type** | method / alternative-explanation / so-what / generalization / AI-use / other |
| **Target** | **claims** (about whether your result is true/bounded) vs **communication** (about whether the poster was clear) |
| **Recurrence** | how many times you got a version of it |

The claims-vs-communication split is the key move: a communication question means
fix the poster; a claims question means the evidence itself was probed. They lead
to different fixes — a poster tweak versus a claim you must re-scope — so tag them
apart.

## Finding your pattern

Once coded, ask the tally three questions:
1. Which **type** dominated? (Your project's most-probed flank.)
2. Was the most recurrent question about **claims** or **communication**?
3. What did visitors ask that your hot seats never predicted — and why did the
   class miss it?

The answer to (2) is usually the headline of your reflection: it tells you
whether Nov 17 challenged your evidence or your clarity.

## Procedure (50 minutes)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–8 | **Story round:** 90 seconds each — the single most surprising moment at your poster. |
| 8–15 | Mini-lesson: feedback is data — code it (recurred? from whom? claims or communication?). |
| 15–32 | **Coding studio:** log every question into the nb12 tally (type, target, recurrence); find your pattern. |
| 32–44 | **Pair analysis:** swap tallies — tell your partner what their pattern says that they can't see. |
| 44–48 | **Reflection freewrite:** the honest paragraph — what the conference changed in how you see your project. |
| 48–50 | Exit ticket (the most repeated question + what its repetition evidences); M12 due tonight. |

## Roles

- **You:** log honestly (including the questions that stung), code consistently, name your own pattern.
- **Partner (in the swap):** read the tally cold and say the pattern the owner is too close to see.

### Session flow (for the record)

Professor Moreira brings per-poster observation notes from the Expo, checks the
tally template, and if someone missed the Expo for a documented reason, runs the
make-up path (department session or recorded defense) with rehearsal data used
meanwhile. Each person tells one story, codes one tally, reads one partner's.

## Quality checklist (before submitting M12)

- ☐ Every captured question coded on all three dimensions.
- ☐ The claims-vs-communication split applied — not collapsed into one pile.
- ☐ A named pattern, backed by the counts (not a vibe).
- ☐ A partner's outside read incorporated.
- ☐ An honest reflection paragraph — what the Expo actually changed.

## Common failure modes

1. **Nothing captured.** Un-logged questions are gone by Wednesday. The capture happens Tuesday, in the moment.
2. **Applause as validation.** A warm reception measures your communication, not your claims. Don't code it as proof.
3. **Collapsing claims and communication.** They demand different fixes; keep them in separate columns.
4. **Pattern by vibe.** "People liked it" is not a pattern. Name the count that supports the claim.
5. **Cherry-picking the flattering questions.** The stinging, recurrent one is the data you most need.
