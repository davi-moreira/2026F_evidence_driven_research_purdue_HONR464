# Instructor Intervention Protocol for SRL Sessions

**(Instructor-only; not published to students.)**

*How to support a Student Research Lead without taking the room from them. The
SRL owns the session; the instructor is the accuracy backstop, the pacing
metronome, and the live grader. The governing instinct is minimal, deliberate
intervention: step in on purpose, for a reason on this page, then hand the room
straight back.*

---

## The stance

The SRL is running a real investigation, and investigations wobble. A wobble is
not a failure to rescue. Most silences, wrong turns, and detours are where the
learning happens, and a lead who is never allowed to recover never learns to
lead. Intervene when accuracy, safety, inclusion, or the clock genuinely require
it, and not before. When in doubt, wait five more seconds than feels
comfortable.

Every intervention below is designed to preserve the lead's authority. The room
should read each one as the instructor helping the lead succeed, never as the
instructor replacing the lead.

## The four interventions

### 1. A conceptual error is spreading — "flag, don't grab"

When a wrong idea is taking hold in the room and the lead has not caught it,
flag the error without seizing the session.

- **Do:** name that something needs a second look and route it back through the
  lead. "Hold on — before we build on that, is that actually right? Take us
  back to it." The lead re-opens the question; the room does the correcting.
- **Do not:** deliver the correct answer from the back of the room. Grabbing the
  concept ends the investigation and trains the room to wait for the instructor.
- **Escalate only if** the error survives a second pass. Then correct it
  directly, briefly, and hand the room back: "That one I will settle — here is
  why — now keep going."
- The Monday **31–43 formalization block** is the designed place for the
  authoritative version. Prefer to hold a non-urgent correction for that block
  rather than interrupt the 9–31 investigation.

### 2. The room goes dead — seeded cold-calls

When participation collapses and the lead cannot restart it, seed the room back
to life.

- Offer a low-stakes, named cold-call that reopens the lead's own question:
  "Let me put [name] on the spot — what did you commit to before we opened
  Gemini?" Committed written answers make this safe, because everyone already
  has something to read.
- Hand the thread back immediately: "Good — [lead], run with that." Do not chain
  a second cold-call; one is a jump-start, two is a takeover.
- If the room is dead because the puzzle did not land, note it for the prep
  debrief rather than fixing the puzzle live.

### 3. An AI failure the lead misses — "ask the lead to ask"

When the AI produces a fabrication, a plausible-but-wrong method, or a
missing-uncertainty answer, and the lead moves past it, do not point out the
failure yourself. Point the lead at it.

- Prompt the lead to run the interrogation: "Before we accept that — [lead],
  ask the room whether that source is real." The lead asks; the room hunts.
- This preserves the lead's role as the interrogator and rehearses the exact
  move the rubric grades in rows 4 and 5.
- Only name the failure directly if the lead cannot pick up the thread after the
  nudge, and even then, frame it as a shared find: "Here is what to watch for —
  the citation looks invented. How would we confirm?"

### 4. Timing overrun — published checkpoint signals

The section frames are fixed and enforced by the session-guide generator. The
instructor is the visible clock so the lead does not have to watch it.

- **Monday checkpoints at minutes 9, 31, 43.** Signal at each: 0–9 puzzle and
  commitment, 9–31 guided AI investigation, 31–43 human verification and
  instructor formalization, 43–50 decision and defense.
- **Wednesday checkpoints at minutes 7, 30, 42.** Signal at each: 0–7 retrieval
  and challenge, 7–30 applied AI lab, 30–42 peer defense, 42–50 transfer to
  projects.
- Use a consistent, quiet signal (a raised card, a note on the board, a two-word
  call). A predictable checkpoint lets the lead self-correct pace without losing
  face.
- If a block runs long, protect the closing decision and defense at all costs.
  A session that never lands on a defended human decision has missed the point,
  so cut the middle before you cut the end.

## Grading live with the rubric

Score `srl_rubric.md`'s nine rows in real time, on a printed or on-screen copy,
during the session itself. Waiting until afterward loses the specific moments the
score should rest on.

- **Watch the two owned blocks most closely.** On Monday that is 0–9 and 9–31;
  on Wednesday, 0–7 and 7–30. The formalization block (Monday 31–43) is the
  instructor's, so weigh it lightly in the lead's score.
- **Note moments, not impressions.** Jot the actual question that landed (row 2),
  the assumption surfaced (row 3), the AI interrogation observed or missed (row
  5), and how a wrong answer was handled (row 9). Specific notes make feedback
  usable and grades defensible.
- **Apply the hard cap deliberately.** If the lead presents an AI answer as
  settled without verification, row 4 (Productive use of AI) is capped at
  Beginning regardless of the rest. This is the course discipline with teeth;
  do not soften it, but do tell the lead exactly what triggered it.
- **Return the score with two or three concrete next steps**, timed so the lead
  has them before their next slot. The next slot, not a revision, is the
  improvement window.

## Prep-review touchpoints

The score is shaped before the session, at two fixed touchpoints.

- **Five days out:** release the instructor notebook's SRL page for the slot to
  the assigned lead. It carries the concept in focus, the compass position or
  design of the week, a seed puzzle, and the one thing the room must leave
  understanding. Confirm the lead received it and knows their format (Monday or
  Wednesday) from the Schedule page.
- **Two days out:** the lead submits the preparation template
  (`srl_prep_template.md`). Review it for the load-bearing pieces: a puzzle with
  a real answer and tempting wrong ones, a genuine commit-before-AI moment,
  specific Gemini prompts with honest predictions of what the AI will get wrong,
  a real assumption-probe, a closing decision, and a fallback if the tool is
  down. Return notes the lead can act on. A weak template is the cheapest
  possible place to fix a session.
- **No template two days out** triggers the escalation in
  `absent_lead_protocol.md`.
