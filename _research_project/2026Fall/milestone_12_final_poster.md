# Milestone 12 — Final Poster

## About the Research Project

Your semester project is **individual**: one researcher, one question, carried
from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones
M00–M23, peaks publicly at the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo
poster session (Tuesday, November 17 — required)**, and closes with an oral
**Evidence Defense** and a final research dossier in December. Every milestone
follows the same cadence: **Friday-studio kickoff → develop → present → submit
→ revise (where eligible)**.
Milestone weights and the revision policy are in the syllabus; instructions
and rubrics live one page per milestone, like this one.

This milestone — the **final poster** — is worth **8% of the course grade**, the
largest single slice of the 20% *URC poster & presentation* component
(storyboard 2% → draft + red-team 3% → final poster 8% → Expo presentation 7%).
It is a **terminal artifact**: **the deadline governs and there is no post-hoc
revision.** Friday, November 6 is the real production deadline, not a buffer —
the poster you submit is the poster strangers read on November 17.

## Purpose

Everything the poster milestones have built converges here: the storyboard's
read path, the draft's figures, the red-team's fixes — assembled into a single
print-ready artifact that has to stand on its own in front of an audience you
will not get to coach. This is a **production** milestone, not a design one. The
creative decisions were made at M10 and M11; today you execute them to spec and
verify that every claim on the board is one you can defend to a stranger.

The verification bar is specific and non-negotiable: **every number on the
poster traces to a cell in your notebook**, every claim sits inside your
compass position's boundary, the uncertainty is visible on the poster itself, and the
four red-team audits pass. You sign a checklist attesting to this. There is no
"I'll fix it after submission" — after this is the Expo.

## Components

### 1. The print-ready poster (URC specification)

The complete poster, sized and formatted to the URC print spec distributed in
class: dimensions, minimum font sizes at print scale, required identifying
elements. It must be legible at the size it will actually be printed, not just
on your screen.

### 2. Number-to-notebook traceability

**Every number on the poster** — every percentage, estimate, interval, count —
must trace to a specific cell in your analysis notebook that produced it. In
class you demonstrate one such trace to a partner (the pair traceability check);
in principle you must be able to demonstrate any of them.

- ✓ **Traceable:** the poster says "38% (95% CI: 34–42%)"; you can open the
  notebook and point to the cell whose output is 0.38 and the cell that computed
  the interval.
- ⚠️ **Untraceable:** a number you typed from memory, rounded by hand, or
  carried over from an old draft you have since changed. If you can't find the
  cell, the number cannot go on the poster.

### 3. Claim boundary — every claim inside the compass position

Re-check every claim against your declared compass position. A descriptive poster
describes; an associational poster associates; only an identified causal design
says *cause*. The headline especially: the 90-second reader inherits whatever
the headline claims.

- ✓ **Honest headline:** *"Longer commutes coincide with lower reported
  attendance among 812 surveyed Purdue commuters."*
- ⚠️ **Inflated headline:** *"Long commutes make undergraduates skip class."* —
  terminal artifact, live audience, no revision: an overclaim here is one you
  defend at the Expo or recant in public.

### 4. Uncertainty visible on the poster

The uncertainty around your headline must be **on the poster and on the read
path** — an interval, a stated frame limit, a caveat the reader meets before
they leave. Not exiled to a footnote; the 90-second reader never reaches
footnotes.

### 5. The four audits passed

The poster passes all four red-team audits from M11: claim boundary, figure
honesty (no figure's impression outruns its numbers), read path (a stranger
finds the headline in 90 seconds), uncertainty (visible). Your signed checklist
records each as passed.

### 6. Signed final checklist + AI-use disclosure block

The final production checklist, **signed** by you, attesting to the above (print
spec, traceability, boundary, uncertainty, four audits). Plus the standard
AI-use disclosure table: which tool(s), for what task (locate / draft / critique
/ none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is fine; an undisclosed
AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Penalties

- Late: −10 points per day, up to 3 days; not accepted after (documented
  emergencies handled individually per Purdue policy). Because the artifact is
  terminal, a late poster also risks the print timeline for November 17 — the
  deadline is real for logistics, not only for grading.
- Any number on the poster with no notebook source, or any cited source that
  does not exist or does not say what you claim: the Traceability & evidence
  integrity criterion scores Beginning (0–10) regardless of the rest.
- Missing signed checklist or AI-disclosure block: the Verification criterion
  scores 0 and the submission is returned for completion before grading.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **"I'll fix it after submission."** There is no after. The next reader is a
   stranger at the Expo, not a draft grader. Every fix happens before Friday
   11:59 PM.
2. **The screen-legible, print-illegible poster.** It looks crisp on your
   laptop and turns to mush at 36 × 48 inches. Check font sizes at true print
   scale before you submit.
3. **The number you can't find.** A percentage carried from an old draft after
   you re-ran the analysis. If the cell doesn't produce it now, it doesn't go on
   the board.
4. **The footnote uncertainty.** Confident headline, interval hidden in
   six-point type at the bottom. On a terminal poster that reads as hiding it.
5. **The last-minute upgrade.** Under deadline pressure the headline creeps from
   "is associated with" to "leads to." Pressure is exactly when the boundary
   matters most — you defend this sentence out loud in eleven days.
