## Purpose

This is the milestone where your question stops floating and gets a **world** to
live in. Using the MIDA "Model" and "Inquiry" ideas from nb04, you state what
the world would have to be like for your question to have an answer, draw that
world as a small **DAG** (Directed Acyclic Graph), and name the exact quantity
you are after — the number or pattern a genie would hand you if it knew the
truth. Declaring the inquiry *before* any data arrive is what protects you from
inquiry shopping later. At the Friday studio you deliver a 3-minute declaration;
each listener writes you one clarifying question, and you revise from at least
two of them. Alongside the declaration you complete the **Inquiry Declaration**
template (`project/templates/INQUIRY_DECLARATION.md`)
— the standing document that answers "why THIS compass position?" and travels with your
project through M07, M09, and the final dossier.

## Components

### 1. The world / model, in prose

A short paragraph: **what must be true about the world for your question to have
an answer?** Name the key players (the units), the thing that can vary, the
outcome, and the other forces that could plausibly move that outcome. This is
the "possible worlds" idea from nb04 in plain language — you are committing to a
picture of how the world *could* be, not yet claiming which picture is true.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"How do I describe a world for a
> descriptive question — there's no intervention to imagine?"* You still have a
> world: a population, a construct that varies across it, and a data-generating
> story for how you would observe it. A descriptive inquiry's model says "here
> is the population and the quantity that exists in it"; a causal inquiry's model
> adds "and here is the intervention whose effect is defined by comparing
> potential outcomes." Both are models; they just define different quantities.

**A worked example of a model in prose:**

> Consider a mentoring program. My units are first-year undergraduates in the
> Honors College. Whether a given person had a mentor can vary. The outcome I
> care about is their end-of-year sense of belonging. But motivation plausibly
> drives both seeking a mentor *and* belonging — so any raw gap between mentored
> and unmentored individuals mixes the program's influence with who chooses
> mentoring. My world therefore has at least three moving parts: mentoring,
> belonging, and prior motivation.

### 2. A 3–5 node DAG

Draw the world above as a DAG: **nodes** are the variables (cause, outcome, and
one or two others that could confound or explain), **arrows** are the mechanisms
you believe could run between them. For each arrow, be ready to name the
real-world mechanism it asserts (nb04's pair-challenge: could a classmate defeat
your arrow?). Three to five nodes — enough to be honest, few enough to defend.

**What NOT to submit (non-example):**

> A single arrow: *Mentoring → Belonging.* "The effect of mentoring on
> belonging."

That is a claim wearing a model's clothes. With no other node, it silently
assumes nothing else moves belonging — no motivation, no prior connection, no
selection into the program. A one-arrow DAG smuggles in a world you never
argued for. Draw the third node you are tempted to leave out.

### 3. The inquiry — in words AND as a quantity (the genie test)

State the exact thing you want to know two ways: **in one plain sentence a
non-researcher could repeat back**, and **as a quantity** — the number or
pattern the genie hands you if it knows the whole truth. Descriptive: a share or
a difference in the population ("the share of units with Y"). Causal: an average
effect defined by comparing potential outcomes ("the average of Y(1) − Y(0)").
Declare it now, before data — that is the whole point.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"Why must I fix the quantity before I
> ever see results?"* Because if you choose the number *after* browsing the data,
> you will — without meaning to — pick the one that looks most exciting and then
> tell yourself it was the question all along. Declaring the inquiry in advance is
> how you keep yourself honest; the quantity you name today becomes the answer key
> you are held to in December.

### 4. Responses to at least two clarifying questions

At the studio, each listener writes you one clarifying question. Choose **at
least two**, and for each write a short response that either sharpens your declaration
or explains your choice. Show the before-and-after: "A listener asked whether
'belonging' meant social or academic belonging; I now specify social belonging,
which narrows my outcome and my later measurement."

### 5. Completed Inquiry Declaration (attached)

Complete the template at `project/templates/INQUIRY_DECLARATION.md`
and attach it as part of this PDF or as an appendix. It requires: your question's
exact wording; your **primary compass position** — Description (descriptive · data
at hand), Generalization (descriptive · population), Prediction (descriptive ·
unseen cases), or Causal reasoning (causal); the **kind + reach** checkboxes that
place it; why that position fits, **naming one neighboring position and why it is
*not* the fit**; what your answer **will** and **will not** be able to establish;
at least three limitations with how you will probe or bound each; and what you
would do if the position turns out wrong. This is the Inquiry-Declaration
Justification rule in writing — a method choice with no stated justification or
limitation is incomplete.

### 6. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The one-arrow DAG.** *Cause → Outcome* with nothing else is not a model; it
   is your conclusion drawn as a diagram. The node you are tempted to omit — the
   confounder, the selection story — is usually the one that matters most.
2. **Inquiry shopping (declared in advance to prevent it).** Naming the quantity
   after you have seen results and presenting it as your original question is the
   exact failure this milestone is built to stop. Fix the quantity now.
3. **A quantity in words but not as a number/pattern (or vice versa).** "I want
   to know if mentoring helps" is not an inquiry until it is also "the average
   difference in belonging between mentored and unmentored individuals." Both
   forms are required.
4. **A compass position with no road-not-taken.** The strongest declarations say
   why the *neighboring* position is wrong for this question. Claiming "causal"
   without arguing why it is not merely "descriptive" leaves the choice unearned.
5. **Limitations that could belong to any project.** "Small sample" and "more data
   would help" are not limitations of *your* design. Name the confounder your DAG
   exposes, the measurement error your outcome invites, the frame your units miss.
6. **Ignoring the clarifying questions.** Two incorporated questions with a
   visible before/after is a graded component — arrive ready to revise, not just
   to defend.
