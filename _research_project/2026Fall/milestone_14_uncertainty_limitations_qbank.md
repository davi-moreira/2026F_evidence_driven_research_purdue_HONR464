## Purpose

At the Expo, a stranger will ask you the hardest fair question about your project,
and how you answer decides whether they leave trusting you. This milestone builds
the two instruments that make that a strength rather than an ambush.

The first is the **uncertainty & limitations statement**, written as the **ULN
move**: *uncertainty → limitation → next step*. Stated first-person and with a
straight spine, a limitation is expertise — it proves you know exactly what your
evidence can and cannot bear. The move has two failure modes and you avoid both:
**hiding** the limitations (dishonest), and **drowning** in them (the apology
spiral — "this is probably all wrong"). A limitation is not an apology; it is the
edge of your claim, drawn by someone who knows where it is.

The second is the **hard-question bank**: the five hardest fair questions a
stranger could ask, one of each recurring type, with your answers prepared —
including one honest *"I don't know."* A prepared "I don't know" is not a
failure; a bluffed answer is the one move that can destroy a presenter's
credibility entirely.

## Components

### 1. The uncertainty & limitations statement (ULN form)

A written statement of what your study cannot show and how much uncertainty
surrounds what it can, structured as **uncertainty → limitation → next step**.
Strength, not apology: each limitation names the boundary and the design move
that would push it, without disowning the finding.

- ✓ **Strong ULN paragraph:** *"My estimate carries a wide interval because the
  sample is 812 commuters from one campus (uncertainty). It is an association in
  a convenience frame — it cannot establish that commuting causes lower
  attendance (limitation). A design that could is a panel that tracks the same
  the same people as their commutes change; that is the next study (next step)."*
- ⚠️ **Apology-spiral non-example:** *"Honestly this is probably all wrong, my
  sample is tiny and biased and I'm not a statistician, so don't read too much
  into any of it."* — this misstates the evidence in the other direction: it
  hides a real, defensible finding behind self-erasure and tells the visitor
  nothing about where the claim actually stops.

### 2. The 5-question hard-question bank (one per type)

The five hardest fair questions for **your** poster, one of each type, each with
an **answer sketch**:

1. **Method** — "Why this analysis and not another?"
2. **Alternative explanation** — "Couldn't X explain your result instead?"
3. **So-what** — "Why does this matter?"
4. **Generalization** — "Does this hold beyond your sample?"
5. **AI-use** — "What did you use AI for, and how do you know it's right?"

At least one answer sketch must be an **honest don't-know** in the full form:
*"I don't know — but here is what I do know that bears on it, and here is how one
would find out."* An honest don't-know has content; it is not a shrug.

- ✓ **Honest don't-know:** *"I don't know whether it holds for graduate-level peers
  — my frame was undergraduates. What I do know is the association was stable
  across the three undergraduate years I have; to find out, you'd re-run the
  survey on a graduate sample."*
- ⚠️ **The bluff:** *"Oh, it definitely generalizes to everyone."* — a claim you
  cannot support, offered to avoid saying you don't know. This is the credibility
  killer the bank exists to prevent.

### 3. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine entry;
an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation. (Note: your
own AI-use question in the bank is about the *project*; this block is about *this
document* — keep them distinct.)

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The apology spiral.** "This is probably all wrong." Drowning in limitations
   misstates the evidence exactly as much as hiding them does. Say what the study
   *can* show, then where it stops.
2. **The hidden limitation.** Omitting the frame limit and hoping no one asks.
   Someone will ask, and the omission looks worse than the limit ever would.
3. **The soft bank.** Writing five questions you can already answer easily. The
   bank only helps if the questions are the ones you fear.
4. **The bluff.** Inventing an answer to dodge "I don't know." A prepared,
   contentful don't-know reads as confidence; a bluff, once caught, sinks
   everything you said.
5. **The next-step-less limitation.** Naming what the study can't do without
   naming the design that could. The next step is what turns a limitation from
   an apology into a research agenda.
