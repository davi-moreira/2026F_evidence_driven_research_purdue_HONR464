## Purpose

M01 gave you one question resting on 1–3 sources you actually examined. This
milestone turns that toehold into a **map of what your field asserts and on what
evidence** — and pins your gap to a spot on that map where no verified source
already sits. The discipline that makes this milestone matter is **citation
integrity**: every source on your ledger is one *you personally retrieved and
read*, whose central claim you can state in your own words. In nb03 you
practiced verifying real citations end to end and learned the tells of
fabricated ones; here you turn that verification reflex on your own reading
list. At the Friday studio your map goes on a
screen, the class circulates with sticky-note challenges, and you incorporate
one challenge live. The map you leave with is the backbone M03 builds its model
and inquiry on.

## Components

### 1. Verified source ledger (at least 6 sources)

A table of **at least six sources you personally retrieved**. For each: a full
citation, *where you retrieved it* (database, journal, repository — not "an AI
told me about it"), its **central claim in your own words**, and the **evidence
type** behind that claim (descriptive / inferential / predictive / causal). A
source you only read an AI's summary of does not count and must not appear.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"An AI tool gave me a perfect-sounding
> source I can't find anywhere — do I list it?"* No — and this is the whole
> lesson. A citation you cannot retrieve is either hallucinated or misremembered;
> either way it is not evidence. Log the search you ran, note that it failed
> verification, and move on. The retrieval-verification loop from nb03 is exactly
> this: found → retrieved → read → claim stated. Skip a step and the source is
> not yet real.

**A worked example of one ledger row:**

> **Source:** [author, year, article title, journal, vol/pages] — *retrieved
> from Purdue Libraries database, PDF read in full.* **Central claim (my
> words):** In this LAPOP survey wave, self-reported trust in Brazil's elected
> institutions is markedly lower than trust in the armed forces. **Evidence
> type:** descriptive (a pattern in one survey's data, not a causal or
> population-general claim).

That row proves retrieval, states the claim plainly, and types the evidence
honestly — it does not inflate a descriptive survey pattern into "Brazilians
distrust democracy."

**What NOT to submit (non-example):**

> *Smith et al. (2019) show that mentoring programs improve first-year retention.
> (Source recommended by an AI assistant; abstract looked right.)*

This fails on every axis: no retrieval location, a claim lifted from a summary
you did not read, no evidence type, and "improve … outcomes" quietly upgrades
whatever the study actually found into a causal promise. If the paper cannot be
retrieved and read, it is not on your ledger.

### 2. The claim map (from nb03's builder)

Using nb03's claim-map builder, fill the claims table and run the graph cell so
the map draws itself. **Nodes are claims** (drawn from your verified ledger);
**edges mark agreement or contradiction** between them. The point is to *see*
the shape of the conversation — where sources cluster, where two of them
genuinely disagree, and where the map goes quiet.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"Two of my sources flatly
> contradict each other — is one of them wrong?"* Often neither is. Two studies
> can reach opposite findings because they sampled different populations,
> measured the construct differently, or used different designs. Draw the
> contradiction edge and, in a sentence, name the design difference that could
> explain it. A contradiction you can explain is a gap you can occupy.

### 3. Your gap, placed on the map

Mark where your gap sits — a spot the map shows is genuinely open — and state it
in the **bounded** form: *"Based on the sources I examined, ___ has not been
established for ___."* Name the gap's **type** (evidence / population /
measurement / method / contradiction). The map is your evidence that the gap is
real: no verified node already covers it.

### 4. One incorporated peer challenge

During the gallery walk, the class leaves sticky-note challenges on your map.
Pick one, incorporate it into the map or the gap statement *before you submit*,
and add a one- or two-sentence note naming the challenge and what you changed.
"A classmate showed my source #4 already covers the gap I placed, so I moved the
gap to the population my sources never reach" is exactly the move.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation. If AI
helped you *locate* a source, the ledger must still show you retrieved and read
it yourself.

## Penalties

- Late: −10 points per day, up to 3 days; not accepted after (documented
  emergencies: talk to me first, per the syllabus).
- **Evidence-integrity rule with teeth:** any source on your ledger that turns
  out not to exist, or not to say what you claim it says, sends the **Source
  ledger & evidence integrity** criterion to Beginning (0–12) regardless of the
  rest of the ledger's quality. This is the course's central habit; here it is
  enforced, not encouraged.
- Missing AI-disclosure block: Craft scores 0 and the submission is returned for
  completion before grading.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The unread source.** An AI summary of a paper is not the paper. If you did
   not open and read it, it cannot sit on your ledger — and one fabricated or
   misrepresented entry caps the whole ledger criterion at Beginning.
2. **Claims that overreach their evidence type.** "This survey shows citizens
   distrust democracy" turns a descriptive pattern into a sweeping generalization.
   Type each claim by the evidence actually behind it, and word it to match.
3. **A gap a listed source already fills.** The map exists to catch exactly this.
   If a node covers the spot where you placed your gap, the gap is not open —
   move it to where the map is genuinely quiet.
4. **"The literature proves…"** A map of claims is not proof, and unread sources
   count for nothing. The map shows what is *asserted*, and by whom, on what
   evidence — not what is settled.
5. **A decorative map.** Nodes with no edges, or edges you cannot trace back to a
   ledger claim, mean the map was drawn, not built. Every node and edge should
   answer to a row in your ledger.
6. **Ignoring the sticky notes.** The gallery walk is not a formality; one
   incorporated challenge is a graded component. Arrive expecting to change
   something before you submit.
