## Purpose

M03 declared the world your question lives in and the quantity you seek; M04
pinned down how you would *measure* it. This milestone answers the next
question every design must survive: **where do the data actually come from, and
can they exist by November without harming anyone?** This is the **D** of MIDA
made concrete and made honest. You state who gets into your data and who is
silently left out, how units come to receive whatever they receive, whether the
whole plan is buildable in one undergraduate semester, and what you owe the
people your design touches. A beautiful question with no reachable data — or
data you cannot ethically obtain — is not yet a research project. This milestone
is where that gets caught early, while there is still time to pivot.

## Components

### 1. Sampling paragraph — the frame, the sample, the population (≈1 paragraph)

Name three different things and keep them distinct: the **population** your
claim wants to speak about, the **sampling frame** you can actually reach, and
the **sample** you will draw from that frame by a stated **procedure** (simple
random, stratified, convenience, a full census of an existing file). Then name,
in one sentence, **who is missed** — the units that fall outside your frame and
whether that exclusion likely correlates with your outcome.

- **Example (grounded in course data).** "My population is registered voters in
  the parish; my frame is `la_voter_file.csv`, the county's registration file;
  my sample is all rows in the file (a census of the frame). Missed: eligible
  residents who never registered — disproportionately younger and more mobile
  than the file, which will pull my turnout picture older than the electorate."
- **Non-example.** "I surveyed the people on my dorm floor, so this tells us
  what the whole campus thinks." A convenience sample narrated as a population
  fact. The floor is the frame; "the whole campus" is a population it does not
  reach.

### 2. Assignment-or-selection paragraph (≈1 paragraph)

State how units come to be in the groups you compare. If your project
**intervenes**, describe the **assignment** procedure (random or not) and what
it buys you. If your project is **observational**, describe the **selection**
process that sorted units into the conditions you observe, and name the most
likely reason that selection is *not* random — the quiet confounding you will
have to reason around later.

- **Example.** In the recurring **mentoring-program simulated world** used in
  class, treatment is assigned by a coin flip, so the mentored and un-mentored
  groups are comparable in expectation — random assignment is doing the work.
- **Non-example.** "People who chose to attend tutoring scored higher, so
  tutoring works." Here selection, not assignment, sorted the groups; the people
  who chose tutoring may have differed before any tutoring happened.

### 3. Feasibility triage (four lines)

Score your design on the four feasibility questions, one honest line each:

- **Data by when?** Can the data you need exist by the pilot (M09, Oct 30) and
  the poster (M12, Nov 6)? Name the date the data would be in hand.
- **Skills?** Does the analysis require anything beyond the provided course
  machinery? (All machinery is provided; if your answer is "yes," reconsider.)
- **Cost?** Any money, software, or access required — and how it is covered.
- **Consent?** Does obtaining the data require anyone's permission?

### 4. Ethics paragraph — decision-tree outcome (≈1 paragraph)

Walk the ethics decision tree from nb07 and state your outcome: **secondary
data / survey / intervention**. Give the **minimal-risk reasoning** for your case.
**Secondary data (already-collected, de-identified) is the honors-scale default
for this course** — reach for it first. If your design is a survey or an
intervention involving human subjects, state why it is minimal-risk and how
consent is handled. Anything that would require formal **IRB review must already
have been escalated in office hours** before this submission — the paragraph
then records that conversation and its resolution (typically a pivot to
secondary data). "It's just a class project, so ethics doesn't apply" is not an
available answer.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate sources /
operationalize / draft / critique / none), and how you verified the output.
"No AI used" is a fine entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an
academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Frame–population fusion.** Writing as if the file you can reach *is* the
   population. Name the gap between them; that gap is your future limitations
   paragraph.
2. **The invisible excluded.** Every frame misses someone. If you cannot say who
   your data leaves out, you cannot say who your claim is about.
3. **The November-impossible design.** An original survey that needs three waves
   of recruitment cannot pilot by Oct 30. Feasibility is a design constraint,
   not a formality — default to secondary data.
4. **Ethics as afterthought.** Deciding "minimal risk" without walking the tree,
   or discovering an IRB need the week of the pilot. Escalate early.
5. **Sampling and assignment confused.** Random *sampling* decides who you see;
   random *assignment* decides what they get. They answer different questions —
   keep them in separate sentences.
