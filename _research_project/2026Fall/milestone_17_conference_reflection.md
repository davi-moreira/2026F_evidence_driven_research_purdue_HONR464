## Purpose

At the Expo, strangers asked you questions your friends and the class hot seats
never thought to ask. That is not noise to be flattered or bruised by — it is
**data about your project**, collected from the one audience you cannot
assemble in a classroom. This milestone makes you treat it that way: code every
question you received like you would code any dataset, find the pattern in it,
and let a partner read your tally back to you so you see what your own eyes
skipped. The discipline being trained is separating **communication signal**
(they didn't follow the read path) from **claims signal** (they doubted what
the evidence supports) from **noise** — and refusing to mistake a warm
reception for validation of your claims. A crowd can love a poster whose central
claim is wrong.

## Components

### 1. The coded question tally

Every question and reaction you received at the Expo, logged as data. Each row
records three codes:

- **Type** — what kind of question was it? (e.g., clarifying, scope/
  generalization, mechanism/"why", so-what/implication, method challenge,
  data-source question, hostile/skeptical).
- **Target** — did it aim at your **claims** (whether the evidence supports what
  you say) or at your **communication** (whether a listener could follow you)?
- **Recurrence** — how many times, and from whom (faculty, graduate researchers,
  peers, general public)? A question asked once is an anecdote; a question asked
  three times is a finding about your project.

**Strong row (concrete example):** *"How would this look for transfer undergraduates?
— type: scope/generalization; target: claims; recurrence: asked 3× (two
faculty, one peer)."* It is codeable, and its recurrence count means something.

**Weak row (non-example):** *"People seemed to like it."* This is not a coded
question — no type, no target, no recurrence. A page of these is applause
transcription, not audience data.

### 2. The pattern you found

State, in a sentence or two grounded in the tally, the **recurring pattern**:
the question or concern that came back most, and which part of your project
(the claim, the figure, the framing, the scope) it points at. The pattern must
be readable off your own table — cite the rows.

### 3. What your partner saw that you couldn't

In class you swap tallies with a partner and read each other's patterns. Record
what your partner noticed in **your** tally that you had missed — a cluster you
under-weighted, a "communication" question you had mis-coded as a "claims"
question, a recurrence you hadn't counted. Blind spots in reading your own
reception are the point here; name at least one honestly.

### 4. The honest reflection paragraph

One paragraph: **what the conference changed in how you see your project.** Not
"it went well" or "it was stressful" — what you now believe differently about
your question, your evidence, or your claim's boundary because of what the
audience data showed you. If the honest answer is "the audience confirmed my
scope was too narrow," say that. If it is "warm reception, but no one probed my
weakest claim, so I still don't know if it holds," say that too — that is the
most useful reflection of all.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Applause as evidence.** A warm reception measures how well you
   *communicated*, not whether your *claims* are true. Code the two separately
   and never let the first stand in for the second.
2. **The un-coded tally.** A bulleted memory of the day is not data. If you
   can't sort each row by type, target, and recurrence, you haven't coded it.
3. **Counting only the compliments.** The skeptical question you'd rather forget
   is the highest-value row in your table. Log it first.
4. **A reflection that reflects nothing.** "It was a great experience" tells your
   project nothing. Say what you now believe differently, and why.
5. **Ignoring your partner.** If your partner's read didn't change a single code
   or count, you probably defended your tally instead of reading it.
