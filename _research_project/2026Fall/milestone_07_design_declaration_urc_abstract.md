# Milestone 07 — Design Declaration + URC Abstract

## About the Research Project

Your semester project is **individual**: one researcher, one question, carried
from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones
M00–M23, peaks publicly at the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo
poster session (Tuesday, November 17 — required)**, and closes with an oral
**Evidence Defense** and a final research dossier in December. Every milestone
follows the same cadence: **Friday-studio kickoff → develop → present → submit
→ revise (where eligible)**.
Milestone weights and the revision policy are in the syllabus; instructions
and rubrics live one page per milestone, like this one.

## Purpose

Everything since M03 has been built one piece at a time. This milestone assembles
the pieces and shows them to the world. Part A, the **URC abstract**, is your
project's first public commitment — 150–250 words a stranger could read and
understand, already carrying uncertainty in its language because your findings
will. Part B, the **full design declaration**, distills M03 through M06 onto a
single page: Model, Inquiry, Data strategy, Answer strategy, coherent and
consistent, plus the justification for the compass position you chose. Together
they are the blueprint every later milestone builds on — the diagnosis (M08),
the pilot (M09), and the poster (M10–M12) all read back to this page.

## Components

### Part A — the URC abstract (150–250 words)

A single self-contained paragraph (or two) that moves through: the **motivation
and gap**, the **research question**, the **compass position and design** in one phrase,
what the project **will show**, and — woven in, not appended — **uncertainty-
aware language**. The reader who sees only your abstract should come away knowing
what you are asking, how you are answering it, and how much to believe you.

- **Example (grounded in course data).** A trust-in-government project using the
  `lapop_brazil` items might write: "Using survey responses from Brazil, this
  project estimates how reported institutional trust differs across age groups.
  Because the data are a resampled cross-section, estimates are reported with
  uncertainty intervals and describe association, not cause." Uncertainty and
  claim boundary are already in the prose.
- **Non-example.** "This project proves that older Brazilians trust the
  government more." A bare, causal-sounding certainty — the exact "the effect IS
  4.2" overclaim nb10 rules out. The abstract inflates what the design can buy.

### Part B — the full design declaration (one page)

Assemble the four MIDA letters on one page, each in a short block:

- **Model** — the world your question lives in: the DAG or potential-outcomes
  setup from M03 (cause, outcome, the key third variable).
- **Inquiry** — the estimand: the exact quantity, defined before data.
- **Data strategy** — sampling, assignment/selection, and the feasibility +
  ethics outcome from M05, compressed to their essentials.
- **Answer strategy** — the estimator, uncertainty plan, and boundary-safe
  result sentence from M06.

### Component C — inquiry-declaration justification (inside the declaration)

State, at a conceptual level, for the compass position you declared: **(1) why it
fits** your question, **(2) what it can and cannot establish**, and **(3) its key
limitations and sources of uncertainty**. A method choice with no stated
justification or limitation is incomplete — this is the course's
Inquiry-Declaration rule, and it is graded here.

### Component D — AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate sources /
operationalize / draft / critique / none), and how you verified the output.
"No AI used" is a fine entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an
academic-integrity violation. Include it on **both** the abstract and the
declaration.

## Penalties

- Late: −10 points per day, up to 3 days; not accepted after (documented
  emergencies: talk to me first, per the syllabus). The **abstract gate is hard**
  — a late abstract still submits to Brightspace but forfeits the workshop credit
  and cannot be revised.
- An abstract that states a bare certainty or a causal claim the design cannot
  buy: the Abstract criterion scores Beginning (0–10).
- Any cited source or dataset that turns out not to exist or not to say what you
  claim: the relevant criterion scores Beginning — the course's
  evidence-integrity rule with teeth.
- Missing AI-disclosure block on either file: Craft scores 0 and that file is
  returned for completion before grading.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The certainty abstract.** Compressing a project into confident-sounding
   prose by deleting every hedge. The uncertainty is the finding's honesty —
   keep it in the abstract, not just the paper.
2. **The declaration that contradicts itself.** A causal inquiry up top and an
   association-only estimator below. Read the four blocks as one argument before
   you submit.
3. **Justification without limits.** Saying why your compass position fits but never what
   it *cannot* establish. The limitation is half the justification.
4. **Missing the gate.** Treating Oct 9 as soft because the external URC date is
   TBD. The internal gate binds; the abstract is not revisable.
5. **Recycling M06 verbatim.** The declaration distills — it does not paste. One
   page means choosing the essential sentence from each earlier milestone.
