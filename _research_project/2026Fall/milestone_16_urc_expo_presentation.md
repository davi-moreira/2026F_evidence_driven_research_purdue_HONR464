# Milestone 16 — URC Expo Presentation (required)

## About the Research Project

Your semester project is **individual**: one researcher, one question, carried
from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones
M00–M23, peaks publicly at the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo
poster session (Tuesday, November 17 — required)**, and closes with an oral
**Evidence Defense** and a final research dossier in December. Every milestone
follows the same cadence: **Friday-studio kickoff → develop → present → submit
→ revise (where eligible)**.
Milestone weights and the revision policy are in the syllabus; instructions
and rubrics live one page per milestone, like this one.

This milestone — the **URC Expo presentation** — is worth **7% of the course
grade**, the final slice of the 20% *URC poster & presentation* component
(storyboard 2% → draft + red-team 3% → final poster 8% → Expo presentation 7%).
It is a **terminal, attendance-governed event**: your presence and performance
at the Expo *are* the grade, and there is no revision.

## What to Submit on Brightspace

**There is no file to upload for the presentation itself.** This milestone is a
**live event**: the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo, Tuesday,
November 17**, at your assigned session. Your grade comes from Professor Moreira's
**observation rubric**, scored at your poster during the session. Expo
logistics (your session assignment, setup, and what to bring) are covered at

One thing you carry *out* of the Expo matters for the next milestone: **every

questions as data, so a session with no captured questions loses the raw
material for the reflection that follows.

## Purpose

This is what the whole semester built toward: your research, standing in public,
defended by you to strangers who owe you nothing and will believe only what you
can support. The Expo is not a test of whether your project is perfect — no
undergraduate project is. It is a test of whether you can **present honest work
honestly**: deliver the pitch, keep every spoken claim inside the boundary your
poster drew, answer hard questions without bluffing, and represent yourself and
the course professionally for the full session.

It is also the last chance to gather something you can get nowhere else:
**strangers' questions.** Friends and classmates ask the questions they expect;
strangers ask the ones you didn't see coming. Those questions are evidence about
your project, and capturing them is part of this milestone's job.

## Components

### 1. Presence for the full assigned session

You are present, set up, and at your poster for the **entire** assigned session
window — not a drive-by. Presence and professionalism run the whole time,
including when no one is at your poster.

### 2. The poster stood and presented

Your final poster (M12) is displayed to spec and you are actively presenting it —
inviting visitors, delivering pitches, walking the read path.

### 3. Pitches and Q&A performed

You deliver your **1-minute hook** and **3-minute walk** (M13) to visitors as
they come, and you perform the **Q&A** live — fielding questions with the
uncertainty and limitations framing (M14) and the boundary discipline the whole
poster phase trained.

- ✓ **Boundary held under a live question** (visitor: *"So commuting causes the
  attendance drop?"*): *"It's an association in my sample — I can't say it causes
  it. A study that could would track the same people as their commute changes."*
- ⚠️ **The excited-mouth upgrade** (same question): *"Yeah, basically it makes
  them skip."* — the poster says *is associated with*; the answer inflated it to
  a stranger who will repeat it. This is the exact failure the boundary sentence
  exists to prevent.

### 4. Audience questions captured

You **note every question you are asked** — its gist, and if you can, who asked
(a classmate, a faculty visitor, a stranger to the field). This log is not
graded for polish, but its absence is felt Wednesday: M17 codes these questions
to find what recurred, what confused, and what your project taught you that you
couldn't see from inside it.

*(Your poster already carries its AI-use disclosure block from M12; no separate
disclosure is required for the live event. If AI helped you prep answers, a line
in your question log keeps the habit visible.)*

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| **Delivery** (25) | Pitches performed with clarity and command; visitors drawn in and walked well (22–25) | Solid delivery, some flat or rushed stretches (18–21) | Halting or read-off delivery (11–17) | Poster not actively presented (0–10) |
| **Boundary discipline** (25) | Every spoken claim stays inside the poster's boundary under live questions (22–25) | Inside the boundary with one soft moment (18–21) | One clear spoken upgrade past the poster (11–17) | Repeated inflation of the poster's claims (0–10) |

| **Audience-question capture** (10) | Every question captured, usable for Wednesday's coding (9–10) | Most questions captured (7–8) | Only a few noted (4–6) | Nothing captured (0–3) |

## Penalties

- **Absence:** because this is a public event on a fixed date, an unexcused
  absence forfeits the milestone. **Make-up is available only for a documented
  absence** (for example, a scheduled department presentation or a recorded
  defense arranged in advance) — talk to me *before* the Expo whenever possible,
  per Purdue policy.
- **Bluffing a claim** you cannot support, or **inflating the poster's boundary**
  to a live visitor, is scored where it happens (Q&A honesty / Boundary
  discipline) — the credibility cost is the point of grading it.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The drive-by.** Setting up, giving two pitches, and drifting off. Presence
   is the full session — including the quiet stretches.
2. **The excited-mouth upgrade, live.** A stranger's enthusiasm pulls "is
   associated with" up to "causes." Under real interest is exactly when the
   boundary is hardest and most important to hold.
3. **The bluff.** Answering a question you can't support to avoid "I don't know."
   A prepared, honest don't-know (M14) reads as strength; a caught bluff sinks
   the session.
4. **No capture.** Presenting well and noting nothing, then arriving Wednesday
   with no questions to code. The capture is half of why the Expo feeds the rest
   of the course.
5. **Under-dressing the work.** Treating a public research conference like a
   class check-off. Professionalism — dress, engagement, courtesy to visitors —
   is graded because it is part of representing the work.
