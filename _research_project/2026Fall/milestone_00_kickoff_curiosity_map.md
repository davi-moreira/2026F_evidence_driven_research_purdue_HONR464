from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones

## Purpose

Every defended claim in December started as a vague itch in August. This
milestone catches three of your itches before they evaporate and gets your
working environment (Colab, Brightspace, the AI-use ledger habit) running so
that no later week is lost to setup. You will pitch one curiosity for 30 seconds
in class on Wednesday; the class gives each pitch one genuine strength and one
honest question. That feedback — and the two curiosities you did *not* pitch —
becomes the raw material M01 narrows into a single answerable question. Breadth
now buys you a good choice later, so resist the urge to pre-commit.

## Components

### 1. Three curiosities (2–3 sentences each)

Write three things you are genuinely curious about — three *different* doors,
not three phrasings of one. For each, say in 2–3 sentences what pulls you in and
what small corner of it you could imagine looking at more closely. A curiosity
is allowed to be broad and unpolished here; the funnel from curiosity to topic
to question is M01's job, not today's.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"Does a curiosity have to be
> 'academic'?"* No. The strongest projects often start somewhere personal — a
> mentoring program you were part of, a claim in a news feed that annoyed you,
> a pattern you noticed in your hometown. What matters is that it is *yours* and
> that, in principle, evidence could speak to some version of it. You will test
> studyability next week; today, chase what actually interests you.

**A worked example of one curiosity (do three like this):**

> *Trust in government.* I keep reading that people in some countries trust
> their governments far less than in others, and I do not understand why the
> same institution earns trust in one place and suspicion in another. I would
> be curious to look at how survey respondents in one country describe their
> trust in specific institutions — courts, elections, police — rather than
> "the government" as one blurry thing.

That entry names the big pull (why trust varies) *and* a smaller corner (trust
in specific institutions, in one country's survey data). It commits to nothing.

**What NOT to submit (non-example):**

> *Curiosity 1: Politics. Curiosity 2: Health. Curiosity 3: Education.*

Three one-word topics are not three curiosities. They tell a reader nothing
about what pulls you or what you might look at, and they give the class nothing
to respond to in your pitch. Open the door; do not just name the building.

### 2. Tool-readiness screenshot (nb00's three ✓ cells)

Open **nb00** in Colab, save a copy to your Drive, and run every readiness cell.
Three of them end in a ✓ when your environment is ready (you can run a cell,
the course data loads, and you have claimed your copy by name). Paste a single
screenshot showing all three ✓ marks into your PDF. This is your proof that no
later milestone will stall on setup. (nb00's fabricated-citation hunt is part
of the notebook too — finish it; your first ledger row below likely comes from
it.)

### 3. Your first AI-use ledger entry

This is the first row of a ledger you keep all semester. Record which AI tool
you used today (if any — running nb00 involves one deliberate encounter with a
fabricated citation), for what task, and how you verified its output. "No AI
used beyond nb00's demo" is a perfectly good entry. This same block satisfies
the disclosure requirement below; from here on, every deliverable carries one.

### 4. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation. For
M00, Component 3 *is* this block — label it as the first ledger row.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Three phrasings of one curiosity.** "Social media and teens," "screens and
   kids," "phones and youth" are one door painted three colors. Open three
   *different* doors — you want real choices at M01.
2. **Pre-writing your whole project.** A polished research question here is not
   rewarded and often boxes you in. Stay broad; narrowing is next week's skill.
3. **Skipping nb00's readiness cells.** If the three ✓ marks are not in your
   screenshot, the tool-readiness points are simply unavailable — and a setup
   problem you hide today becomes a lost class hour in September.
4. **Treating the ledger as paperwork.** The first row is small on purpose so the
   habit is built before the stakes rise. An empty or absent ledger row is the
   one thing that returns the whole submission.
5. **Freezing at the pitch.** Thirty seconds, one curiosity, no slides needed.
   The room is five people being warm to each other; there is no wrong pitch,
   only an unspoken one.
