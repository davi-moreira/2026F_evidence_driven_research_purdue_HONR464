## Purpose

Your poster is finished and it stands on its own. Now you learn to stand beside
it. A visitor at the Expo does not read your poster and then talk to you — they
talk to you *instead of* reading it, and your voice becomes the poster. This
milestone builds the two spoken architectures a poster session demands:

- the **1-minute hook** — the 60 seconds that decide whether a passing visitor
  stops, built as *hook → claim → boundary → invitation*; and
- the **3-minute walk** — the guided tour for the visitor who stopped, that
  moves their eye and yours along the poster's read path.

The iron rule of this milestone is **compression without inflation**. Speaking
is faster than reading, so you will compress — but compression must never
upgrade a claim. The words leaving your mouth must sit inside the exact same
boundary your poster's headline sits inside. The single most common failure of
poster presenters is the excited-mouth upgrade: saying "causes" out loud while
the poster says "is associated with."

## Components

### 1. The 1-minute hook script (boundary sentence marked)

A written script for the 60-second version, following the architecture: a
**hook** that earns attention, the **claim**, the **boundary** that keeps the
claim honest, and an **invitation** to look closer. **Mark the boundary
sentence** in the script — the exact words that hold the claim inside your
compass position. That sentence is the one you rehearse until it is automatic, because it
is the one pressure will try to drop.

- ✓ **Spoken claim that matches the poster:** *"In my sample of 812 commuters,
  longer commutes go hand in hand with lower attendance — this is an
  association, not proof that commuting causes it, and here's the pattern."*
- ⚠️ **The excited-mouth upgrade:** *"Basically, long commutes make people skip
  class."* — the poster says *is associated with*; the mouth said *make*. That
  is the exact word the boundary sentence exists to prevent.

### 2. The 3-minute walk script

A written script for the guided tour: how you move a visitor from the headline
through the evidence hierarchy to the uncertainty and out. It should map onto
your poster's read path, spend its time on the evidence that earns it, and reach
the uncertainty out loud (not skip it because it's less fun to say).

### 3. The poster walk map

A small diagram or annotated list: **what you point at, and when.** For each beat
of the 3-minute walk, which element of the poster your hand is on — headline,
Figure 2, the interval, the limitation. This is what turns a script into a
performance the visitor can follow with their eyes.

### 4. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine entry;
an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The excited-mouth upgrade.** Saying "causes," "proves," or "makes" out loud
   while the poster says "is associated with." Compression is allowed; inflation
   is the one thing that is not.
2. **The read-aloud paper.** A 3-minute walk that recites the poster
   word-for-word. The walk should *guide the eye*, not narrate the text the
   visitor can already read.
3. **The vanishing uncertainty.** Skipping the interval out loud because it is
   less exciting to say. If it's on the poster, it's in the walk.
4. **The unmarked boundary.** No marked boundary sentence means nothing to
   rehearse until automatic — and the boundary is exactly what nerves erase.
5. **The scriptless point.** A walk with no map: you know what to say but not
   what your hand is doing, so the visitor's eyes wander off the read path.
