## Purpose

M17 turned the Expo into coded data. This milestone turns that data into a
**plan**. You take your three most important pieces of feedback and do what a
working researcher does after a conference: map each onto the part of the design
it implicates, propose a real redesign, name the benefit that redesign would
buy, and — crucially — decide what you can fix in the **two weeks that remain**
versus what belongs to the **next study you'll never run for this class**. This
is the moment the semester's `declare → diagnose → redesign` loop closes on
*you*: you diagnose your own design with an audience's help and redesign it on
the record. The line the milestone is built to enforce: **"more data, more
time" is a lament, not a redesign.** A redesign points at the design — at a
Model, Inquiry, Data, or Answer decision you would make differently — and says
what it would improve.

## Components

### 1. Top-3 feedback items, each mapped to a MIDA letter

Choose the three feedback items (from your M17 tally, weighted by recurrence and
importance) that most deserve a response. For each, name the **MIDA letter** it
implicates:

- **M**odel — the world/assumptions/mechanism (a "why does that happen?"
  question usually lives here).
- **I**nquiry — the quantity or question itself (a "so what?" or "is that even
  the right thing to measure?" question).
- **D**ata — sampling, measurement, assignment, sources (a "who was in your
  sample?" or "how did you measure that?" question).
- **A**nswer — the analysis and how it maps evidence to the claim (a "would that
  result survive a different method?" question).

### 2. A proposed redesign per item, with its diagnosed benefit

For each of the three, write the concrete **redesign** and the **diagnosed
benefit** — which design virtue (bias, precision, generalization, mechanism
clarity) it would improve. The redesign must be a *design move*, not a wish.

**Strong item (concrete example):** *"Feedback: 'Your comparison group may
differ on major, not just on the thing you're studying' (asked twice). →
Letter D. Redesign: measure college/major as a covariate and report the
comparison within-major, or stratify the sample. Diagnosed benefit: removes a
confound that currently caps my claim at a within-one-major boundary. Triage:
NOW — I can add the major field to this week's recode."*

**Weak item (non-example):** *"Feedback: people wanted more responses. Redesign:
get more data. Triage: NEXT."* This names no MIDA letter, points at no design
decision, and diagnoses no benefit beyond "bigger" — it is the lament the
milestone exists to catch.

### 3. NOW vs NEXT triage

Tag every redesign **NOW** or **NEXT**:

- **NOW** — it fits inside the remaining two weeks and therefore **enters your
  dossier work plan** (M19–M23). A NOW item is a commitment; list it where your
  December work can find it.
- **NEXT** — it is real and worth stating, but belongs to the imagined next
  study. Naming what you *would* do next is part of a mature redesign; pretending
  you can do it in two weeks is not.

Be honest about the boundary. A NOW item you can't actually finish becomes a
broken promise in your dossier; a NEXT item mislabeled NOW is optimism, not a
plan.

### 4. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **"More data, more time."** The signature failure. If your redesign could be
   written on a fortune cookie, it isn't one. Point at a Model, Inquiry, Data,
   or Answer decision.
2. **Redesign as regret.** "I wish I'd started earlier" is a feeling, not a
   plan. Convert the feeling into a design move or drop it.
3. **Everything is NOW.** Tagging three ambitious redesigns NOW guarantees at
   least two broken promises. Triage like the calendar is real, because it is.
4. **Mapping to the wrong letter.** A "who was in your sample?" question is
   almost always **D**, not **I**. Mis-mapping hides the real fix.
5. **Diagnosed benefit missing.** A redesign with no stated benefit can't be
   prioritized — you don't know what it's worth.
