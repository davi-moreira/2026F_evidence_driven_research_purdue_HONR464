# Milestone 11 — Poster Draft + Red-Team Review

## About the Research Project

Your semester project is **individual**: one researcher, one question, carried
from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones
M00–M23, peaks publicly at the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo
poster session (Tuesday, November 17 — required)**, and closes with an oral
**Evidence Defense** and a final research dossier in December. Every milestone
follows the same cadence: **develop in class → present → submit → revise**.
Milestone weights and the revision policy are in the syllabus; instructions
and rubrics live one page per milestone, like this one.

This milestone — the **poster draft + red-team review** — is worth **3% of the
course grade**, the second slice of the 20% *URC poster & presentation*
component (storyboard 2% → draft + red-team 3% → final poster 8% → Expo
presentation 7%). Part of that 3% is **citizenship**: the rigor of the audits
you give two classmates is graded alongside the draft you submit. Peer review is
the course's quality gate, not a courtesy.

## Purpose

Before a stranger at the Expo finds the weakest thing on your poster, a room of
trained-hostile classmates finds it first — while there is still time to fix it.
That is the whole logic of a red-team: **adversarial review is cheaper than
public embarrassment**. In class you run the four-audit protocol against two
posters, and two reviewers run it against yours. The four audits are the four
ways a poster fails:

1. **Claim boundary** — does any claim outrun the compass position's evidence?
2. **Figure honesty** — does any figure's visual impression say more than the numbers?
3. **Read path** — can a stranger find the headline in 90 seconds?
4. **Uncertainty** — is it visible, or hidden?

The author stays **silent** during the walk (you learn what a real reader sees
when you can't defend yourself), then asks questions. What you submit is the
draft, the hits you took, and your triage: which hits matter most, and the first
one already fixed.

## Components

### 1. The full draft poster

A complete draft — every section populated, real figures from your pilot, the
headline and evidence hierarchy from your M10 storyboard. Build it in any tool
(PowerPoint, Canva, Quarto). "Draft" means finishable by Friday, not skeletal.

### 2. The two audit slips you received

The completed four-audit slips from your two reviewers, included as-received.
Do not edit them — they are evidence of what the review found.

### 3. Your revision triage

Rank the hits your reviewers landed by how much each threatens the poster, then
**apply the top fix now** and describe it: what the reviewer found, why it ranks
first, and exactly what the fixed version says instead. The remaining hits become
your production plan for Friday (M12).

- ✓ **Strong triage:** *"Both reviewers flagged that my headline says 'improves'
  while my design is cross-sectional (claim-boundary audit). Rank 1 — it's the
  most damaging because the whole poster inherits the headline's boundary. Fix
  applied: changed to 'is associated with higher …' and moved the confidence
  interval from the footnote onto the read path. Rank 2: the y-axis on Figure 2
  starts at 40 — I'll zero it Friday."*
- ⚠️ **Non-example:** *"My reviewers didn't really get my project, so I kept the
  draft as is."* — dismissing the audit is the failure. If a trained reader
  misread it, that IS a read-path finding about your poster, not about them.

### 4. Red-team citizenship (the two audits you give — graded)

You red-team **two** classmates' posters with the four-audit slip: specific,
located hits, hard on the poster and warm on the person. A rigorous audit names
*where* and *why*, not just *that*.

- ✓ **Rigorous audit line:** *"Read-path audit: my eye went headline → Figure 3
  → back up to the method box; I never reached your uncertainty line. Move it
  under the headline."*
- ⚠️ **Empty audit line:** *"Looks good, maybe add color."* — no located hit,
  nothing the author can act on; this is the review that earns no citizenship
  credit.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine entry;
an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Draft completeness & compass alignment** (20) | Every section populated, real pilot figures, headline inside the boundary (18–20) | Complete with one thin section or one soft claim (14–17) | Partial draft or a boundary slip (8–13) | Skeletal, or a claim well outside the compass position (0–7) |

## Penalties

- Late: −10 points per day, up to 3 days; not accepted after (documented
  emergencies: talk to me first). A late draft leaves no room before Friday's
  final.
- **No red-team given, no citizenship credit:** if you do not deliver your two
  audits in class, the Red-team citizenship criterion scores Beginning (0–7)
  regardless of your own draft's quality — giving rigorous review is the graded
  obligation, not an extra.
- Any figure or number with no real pilot backing: the Figure honesty & evidence
  integrity criterion scores Beginning (0–7) — the evidence-integrity rule with
  teeth.
- Missing AI-disclosure block: Verification scores 0 and the submission is
  returned for completion before grading.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The unbuilt draft.** You come to class with a storyboard and no poster.
   Your reviewers have nothing to attack, and you enter production day blind. A
   rough full draft beats a polished half.
2. **The defensive author.** Talking during your own red-team walk defeats it —
   you are there to hear what a silent stranger sees, not to explain it away.
3. **The courtesy audit.** "Looks great!" helps no one and earns no citizenship
   credit. A useful audit names the exact spot and the exact fix.
4. **Triage without surgery.** Listing every hit but fixing none. The top hit
   must be repaired now; that is what proves you can take a review and move.
5. **Fixing the cheap hit first.** Reordering colors while the claim-boundary
   error stands. Rank by damage, not by ease.
