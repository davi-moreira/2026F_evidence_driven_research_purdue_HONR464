## Purpose

M03 named the quantity you want. This milestone confronts the fact that the
thing at the heart of that quantity — trust, belonging, well-being, whatever it
is — is an **abstract idea**, and evidence only ever touches **indicators** you
can actually record. Using the concept→construct→indicator ladder from nb05, you
make the leap from idea to number *visible and defensible*, propose at least two
ways to measure your concept and say what each one misses, and confront what
happens to your answer when the measure is a little bit wrong. That last part
draws on nb05's error simulation, where you watched a small **bias** distort a
conclusion more than a large amount of **noise**. On Friday you give a 3-minute
walkthrough; each listener files a two-line review naming your **best rung** and
your **weakest rung**, and you revise from them.

## Components

### 1. Concept → construct → indicator ladder

Climb the ladder for your project's **central concept**. Name the abstract
**concept**, the more specific **construct** you will actually target, and the
concrete **indicator(s)** you could record. Each rung should visibly connect to
the one above it — the reader should see, not just trust, that the indicator
reaches the construct and the construct honors the concept.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"Isn't the concept and the construct
> the same thing?"* Not quite, and the gap between them is where measurement
> lives. "Trust in government" is a concept — broad, contested, felt. A construct
> narrows it to something specific enough to reach: trust in *particular
> institutions* (courts, elections, police) rather than "the government" as one
> blur. The indicator is the actual survey item you would record. Naming a column
> after a concept — labeling a variable "happiness" — does not make it the
> concept; the ladder is how you earn the label.

**A worked example of a ladder:**

> **Concept:** political trust. **Construct:** trust in Brazil's elected
> institutions (as distinct from trust in the armed forces or the church).
> **Indicator:** the LAPOP survey item asking respondents to rate, 1–7, how much
> they trust the National Congress. Each rung narrows the one above it, and the
> indicator is something a real dataset actually contains.

### 2. At least two candidate indicators — with what each misses

Propose **two different indicators** for the same construct, and for each state
**what it misses**. No indicator is the construct; the honest move is to name the
slice each one drops. Two candidates that fail differently are worth more than
one you have talked yourself into.

**What NOT to submit (non-example):**

> **Concept:** undergraduate well-being. **Indicator:** a column named
> `wellbeing` in my dataset. It measures well-being.

This skips the ladder entirely. It never names a construct, offers no second
candidate, says nothing about what the measure misses, and rests its whole case
on the fact that the column is *called* well-being. A name is not a measure.

### 3. Validity and reliability threats

State, in plain language, the main **validity** threat (is the indicator
reaching the right construct at all?) and the main **reliability** threat (would
it give a stable answer on repeat?). The dangerous combination to watch for —
and to check yourself against — is a measure that is highly *reliable* and badly
*invalid*: stable, repeatable, and pointed at the wrong thing.

### 4. The measurement-error paragraph

One tight paragraph, informed by nb05's simulation: **which error type most
threatens your measure — random noise or systematic bias — and how much would it
take to change your story?** In nb05 a 2-point bias moved a conclusion more than
10 points of noise; say which threat is yours and roughly how large it would have
to be to overturn your eventual finding.

> **A question that often comes up here:** *"My measure is clearly imperfect —
> doesn't that sink the project?"* No — and the opposite error ("the measure is
> what it is, ignore the error") is just as wrong. Every measure carries error;
> the researcher's job is to say *which kind*, *which direction*, and *how much
> would matter*. A conclusion that survives the error you can plausibly expect is
> a conclusion you can defend. Bounding the error honestly is the finding, not a
> footnote to it.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine
entry; an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Naming a column after the concept.** A variable called `trust` or `happiness`
   is not trust or happiness. Without the construct and indicator rungs, you have
   renamed the concept, not measured it.
2. **One indicator, dressed as two.** Two phrasings of the same survey item are
   one indicator. The point of the second candidate is that it *fails
   differently* — pick one that misses a different slice.
3. **"Perfectly reliable" mistaken for "valid."** A measure can be beautifully
   stable and still point at the wrong construct. Reliability without validity is
   the most seductive measurement failure; name it before a reviewer does.
4. **The error paragraph that commits to nothing.** "There may be some error" is
   not an answer. Say whether noise or bias is your bigger threat, in which
   direction it runs, and how large it would have to be to flip your conclusion.
5. **Ignoring nb05's asymmetry.** A small bias can outrun a large amount of noise.
   If your measure is vulnerable to systematic error, treating it as mere noise
   understates the danger to your eventual claim.
6. **Skipping the two-line reviews.** Best-rung / weakest-rung feedback is a
   graded component — come ready to move your weakest rung, not just to defend it.
