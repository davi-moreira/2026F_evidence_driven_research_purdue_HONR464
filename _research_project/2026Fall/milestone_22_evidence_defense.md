# Milestone 22 — Evidence Defense

## About the Research Project

Your semester project is **individual**: one researcher, one question, carried
from curiosity to a defended, reproducible claim. It runs through milestones
M00–M23, peaks publicly at the **Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo
poster session (Tuesday, November 17 — required)**, and closes with an oral
**Evidence Defense** and a final research dossier in December. Every milestone
follows the same cadence: **develop in class → present → submit → revise**.
Milestone weights and the revision policy are in the syllabus; instructions
and rubrics live one page per milestone, like this one.

The Evidence Defense is worth **4% of the course grade** (the M44 synthesis
exercise and exit self-assessment add a final 1%, and your cross-examination
questions also feed the engagement component). It is a **terminal performance**:
there is **no post-hoc revision** — only the supported-walkthrough conversion
and optional redo slot described below. The performance is the artifact; there
is no separate PDF.

## Purpose

Everything the semester built points here: not a document, but a **defense**.
For ten minutes you stand behind your **claims, your choices, and your
verification** — why this approach, why this data, why you believe the number —
and for five minutes the class and Professor Moreira **cross-examine** any of
it. A cross-examiner may open your claim ledger to any row, point at any
decision, and ask how you verified any result. This is the moment the course's
whole ethic becomes personal: a claim you can't defend at its boundary was never
yours to make, and a verification you outsourced to an AI tool without checking
is a verification you don't have. The defense rewards **command of your ledger**,
**boundary discipline under pressure**, and the researcher's rarest virtue —
**conceding gracefully exactly where your own ledger says concede**. It is not a
debate you "win" by never yielding; it is a demonstration that you know precisely
how far your evidence reaches.

## Components

### 1. The 10-minute defense (claims, choices, verification)

You present and defend three things, in your own structure:

- **Your claims** — what you found, each stated at its real boundary.
- **Your choices** — why this approach, this data strategy, this measurement,
  this analysis; what each can and cannot establish.
- **Your verification** — how you know each headline result is real (recomputed,
  cross-checked, reproduced by a partner at M20).

Your dossier draft and claim ledger are open in front of you. Lead with the
claims you're proud of, but know where the fragile rows are.

### 2. The 5-minute cross-examination (as defender)

The class and Professor Moreira probe. Expect questions at **any ledger row, any
decision, any verification**. What is graded is not whether you're never pushed
off balance, but how you handle it: hold the boundary where the evidence holds,
and concede where it doesn't.

**Strong move (concrete example):** *"That row is descriptive only. I can't tell
you it generalizes — my sampling frame was one residence hall — so I won't claim
that it does. Within the pilot, the 18-point gap is what I stand on, and here's
the recompute."* Boundary held, concession made where due, verification shown.

**Forbidden moves (non-example) — each costs points:**

- **Inventing a new claim under pressure** — "well, it probably holds nationally
  because…" — a claim with no ledger row, minted to escape a question.
- **Retracting a concession without evidence** — conceding a limit, then walking
  it back when pressed harder, with nothing new to justify the reversal.
- **Blaming the AI tool** — "the AI did that part." You verified, or you didn't;
  the tool is not a co-defendant.

### 3. Cross-examination citizenship (as non-defender)

On both days, when you are **not** defending, you are a cross-examiner. **Every
non-defender must ask at least one ledger-grounded question** — a question aimed
at a specific claim, choice, or verification, not a vague "did you consider…".
This is graded: the defense is a shared adversarial-but-respectful exercise, and
free-riding in the audience is a miss.

## Penalties

- The forbidden moves are scored where they occur: **inventing a new claim under
  pressure** drops Boundary discipline; **retracting a concession without
  evidence** drops Graceful concession; **blaming the AI tool** drops
  Verification & AI accountability — each to Developing or below for that
  criterion.
- Absent from your drawn defense day without a documented emergency: the defense
  cannot be scored (terminal artifact; deadline governs). Documented emergencies
  are handled individually per Purdue policy — talk to me first.
- A non-defender who asks no ledger-grounded question on a defense day scores
  Beginning on Cross-examination citizenship.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Bluffing an answer.** Inventing a claim to escape a hard question is the
   worst move in the room — it costs more than the honest "I don't know; here's
   what I'd need to find out." The ledger is your shield; use it.
2. **Winning instead of defending.** This is not a debate you win by never
   yielding. Conceding where your evidence stops *is* the demonstration of
   competence.
3. **Blaming the tool.** "The AI generated that" is not a defense. You are the
   researcher of record; you verified or you didn't.
4. **Un-conceding under pressure.** Concede once, honestly, and hold it. Walking
   a concession back when pressed harder — with no new evidence — reads as
   defensiveness, not rigor.
5. **Coasting as a cross-examiner.** When you're not defending, you're still
   graded. A vague "did you think about bias?" is not a ledger-grounded question;
   name the row.
