## Purpose

A poster is not a paper stapled to foam board. A stranger gives it **90
seconds**, reads it in whatever order their eye chooses, and leaves with — at
most — one claim. The storyboard is where you decide, before you build anything,
what that one claim is and how the reader's eye will reach it. You are designing
a **read path**: what earns the top-center slot, what supports it, what the
reader is allowed to skip. Get this right and the draft (M11) and final (M12)
are production work. Get it wrong and no amount of color fixes a poster that
tells the reader the wrong thing in the wrong order.

The discipline this milestone trains is **claim economy**: your pilot (M09)
produced more than a poster can hold, and the storyboard forces you to rank what
matters and cut what doesn't — including things you worked hard for.

## Components

### 1. The headline claim (≤ 15 words, inside your compass position's boundary)

The one sentence a 90-second reader must leave with. It must be **worded to
match your declared compass position's claim boundary** (CLAUDE.md Inquiry-Declaration
rule): a descriptive project describes, an associational project associates, and
only an identified causal design says *cause*. The reader will never see your
caveats footnote, so the boundary has to live in the headline itself.

- ✓ **Honest example** (associational pilot): *"In 812 surveyed Purdue
  commuters, longer commutes coincide with lower reported class attendance."* —
  13 words, names the units and the frame, claims association, promises nothing
  it can't defend.
- ⚠️ **Inflated non-example** (same pilot): *"Long commutes cause Purdue
  undergraduates to skip class."* — the pilot was observational; "cause" outruns the
  evidence, and the 90-second reader will believe it because the poster said it.

### 2. Three evidence blocks, ranked by evidential value

The three pieces of evidence that support the headline, ordered so the **most
probative sits closest to the claim**. For each block, name what it is (a figure,
a table, a number) and one line on *why it earns its rank* — what it establishes
that the others don't. Ranking is the point: if you can't say why block 1 beats
block 3, you haven't decided what your poster argues.

### 3. The uncertainty line

One line stating the uncertainty around the headline — the interval, the
sampling frame's limit, the measurement caveat, whichever governs. It belongs
**on the read path**, not exiled to a footnote nobody reaches in 90 seconds.
Mark where on the grid it will sit.

### 4. The read-path arrows

Arrows on the grid showing the order you intend the reader's eye to travel:
entry point → headline → evidence 1 → 2 → 3 → uncertainty → exit. This is what
you will walk a listener down in your 90-second pitch, and where they lose the
thread is exactly what you fix.

### 5. AI-use disclosure block (required on every deliverable)

A short table: which AI tool(s) you used, for what task (locate / draft /
critique / none), and how you verified the output. "No AI used" is a fine entry;
an undisclosed AI contribution is an academic-integrity violation.

**Revision:** this milestone **folds directly into M11**. The lost-point marks
from your 90-second pitch become the fix list for the draft poster you build two
days later; that is where the storyboard's revision happens. The standing 7-day
policy applies in principle, but the poster sequence is deliberately compressed
so you revise by building the next artifact, not by resubmitting this one.

## Penalties

- Late: −10 points per day, up to 3 days; not accepted after (documented
  emergencies: talk to me first, per the syllabus). Note the tight sequence — a
  late storyboard collides with Wednesday's red-team.
- A headline claim that names a source or number that turns out not to exist or
  not to say what you claim: the Evidence criterion scores Beginning (0–10)
  regardless of the rest — the evidence-integrity rule with teeth.
- Missing AI-disclosure block: Verification scores 0 and the submission is
  returned for completion before grading.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **The everything poster.** You put all five pilot outputs on the grid because
   each cost effort. The 90-second reader sees none of them. Rank, then cut the
   bottom two.
2. **The excited headline.** Your mouth wants "causes" because the finding is
   exciting. Your design says "is associated with." The headline must say what
   the design licenses — the whole poster inherits its boundary.
3. **Uncertainty in the basement.** Burying the interval in a bottom-corner
   footnote is the same as hiding it; the reader who leaves at 90 seconds never
   arrives there.
4. **The maze.** Arrows that cross, double back, or offer three entry points.
   If your own read path is ambiguous, a stranger's is impossible.
5. **A claim you can't seat in a block.** If your headline has no evidence block
   that directly supports it, the claim is aspiration, not finding — fix the
   claim, not the color scheme.
