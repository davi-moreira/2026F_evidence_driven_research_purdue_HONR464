Syllabus

HONR 46400: Evidence-Driven Research — Honors College, Purdue University (Fall 2026)

Author

Davi Moreira


IMPORTANT

This document does not replace the official syllabus in the course Brightspace page. It is subject to change.


Course Description and Objectives

This Honors seminar teaches you to turn curiosity into credible, evidence-based insight — no strong background in quantitative methods or computing required. You will learn to identify meaningful gaps in a chosen field, translate a gap into a well-scoped research problem, formulate answerable research questions, classify each question on the inquiry compass — its kind (descriptive vs causal) and its reach (the data at hand, a population beyond the data, or cases not yet seen) — and justify that classification at a conceptual level, and use computational tools and AI to locate sources, operationalize concepts, analyze evidence, verify results, and document decisions responsibly. You will finish able to design and defend a rigorous research approach, interpret findings with appropriate uncertainty and limitations, and communicate results in written and oral formats.

Learning Outcomes

By the conclusion of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Identify a gap and scope a problem.
  2. Formulate answerable research questions.
  3. Classify and justify an inquiry conceptually — its kind (descriptive vs causal) and reach (data at hand / population / unseen cases), and the design that licenses each claim (description, generalization, prediction, causal reasoning).
  4. Locate, appraise, and analyze evidence responsibly with AI, verifying results and documenting decisions.
  5. Interpret and communicate with uncertainty — in written and oral formats.

Assessments

There is no conventional midterm or final exam. The course assesses the research chain itself: a semester-long individual research project carried through milestones M00–M23, presented publicly at the Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo (Tuesday, November 17 — required), and closed with an oral Evidence Defense and a reproducible final research dossier. Grading rewards correctness, transparency, reproducibility, approach-alignment, and responsible interpretation — never coding elegance (no computing background is assumed; all code is provided).

Assessment component Weight
Daily notebook preparation & engagement (incl. Claim Tickets) 15%
Milestone development, presentations & revisions (M00–M09, M13–M15, M17–M18) 30%
URC poster & presentation (M10–M12, M16) 20%
Post-conference final research dossier (M19–M21, M23) 20%
Evidence Defense & course synthesis (M22 + final-week synthesis) 5%
Claim analyses & concept checks (incl. the async claim-diagnosis module) 10%

Revision policy: feedback returns within 3 days of each milestone; most milestones accept a revised version within 7 days of feedback for up to half the lost points (the revising is part of the craft being graded). Terminal artifacts — the final poster (Nov 6), the Expo presentation (Nov 17), the Evidence Defense (Dec 7/9), and the final dossier (Dec 11) — have no post-hoc revision: the deadline governs.

Late policy: −10% per day, up to 3 days, then not accepted (documented emergencies handled individually per Purdue policy).

Grading scale: A ≥ 93, A− ≥ 90, B+ ≥ 87, B ≥ 83, B− ≥ 80, C+ ≥ 77, C ≥ 73, C− ≥ 70, D ≥ 60, F < 60 (no curve).

The Research Project (the course spine)

You will carry one individual research question across the whole semester: from curiosity to gap to answerable question (Aug–Sep), through a full research design declared piece by piece — model, inquiry, measurement, data strategy, answer strategy — then stress-tested by simulation, piloted, and redesigned (Oct), to a poster presented at the Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo (Tue Nov 17, required — the course’s public milestone M16), and finally to a reproducible research dossier defended orally in December. Every milestone follows a develop in class → present → submit → revise cadence, so no deliverable is ever built alone or at the last minute. Milestone instructions and rubrics are posted on Brightspace, one page per milestone (M00–M23). Additional information about Purdue undergraduate research conferences: https://www.purdue.edu/undergrad-research/conferences/index.php.

Grade Challenges

Students who wish to challenge a grade must do so within 3 calendar days of the grade’s release. An exception applies for the last week of class, when assignments must be challenged within 1 calendar day to ensure timely computation of final course grades. Grade challenges must be grounded in legitimate disputes over research-methods principles or grading accuracy. Challenges based on post-hoc legalistic arguments or subjective dissatisfaction will not be considered.

To challenge an assignment score:

  1. Review posted solutions / rubrics thoroughly.
  2. If you still believe an error has been made, email the instructor with:
    • Course name, section, and date/time
    • Your name and Student ID
    • Assignment number / title
    • Specific deduction being challenged
    • Reason for the challenge, with reference to the solutions or grading rubric

Grades will not be discussed in class — before, during, or after class. Please use office hours for questions related to course content or assignment clarification. After the challenge period, all grades are final and cannot be revised further for purposes of calculating final course grades.

Course Policies and Additional Details

AI Policy

I encourage you to use AI tools you believe will enhance your individual or group learning. This course explicitly teaches responsible, documented AI use under the Ask → Verify → Document discipline. Be aware of the following guidelines:

  • Verify everything. AI tools routinely fabricate citations, sources, and facts. Every claim and every source you submit must be independently located and confirmed to exist and to say what you claim it says. Any errors or omissions from your use of an AI tool are your responsibility.
  • Document your use. Include a short disclosure with any deliverable stating which AI tool you used, for what task, and how you verified its output. Undisclosed or unverified AI output is an academic-integrity violation.
  • Use AI to support your learning — ask for explanations, examples, or critiques; do not simply ask for finished answers you cannot defend.
  • Low-effort prompts produce low-quality output. Refine your prompts using the course concepts.
  • You are not permitted to use AI tools during any closed assessment where its use is prohibited.
