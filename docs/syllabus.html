Syllabus

HONR 46400: Evidence-Driven Research — Honors College, Purdue University (Fall 2026)

Author

Davi Moreira


IMPORTANT

This document does not replace the official syllabus in the course Brightspace page. It is subject to change.


Course Description and Objectives

This Honors seminar teaches you to design, analyze, verify, and defend your own empirical research, with no strong background in quantitative methods or computing required. Over one semester you will carry a single project from curiosity to a publicly defended claim. You will find a real gap in a field you care about, scope it into a researchable problem, and ask answerable questions. You will classify each question on the inquiry compass (its kind, descriptive vs causal, and its reach, the data at hand vs a population beyond it vs cases not yet seen) and match it to a design from the research library (observational or experimental, descriptive or causal, with prediction as its own objective). You will declare that design, diagnose it by simulation, redesign it, gather and verify evidence, attack your own analysis, and communicate a claim with its uncertainty and limitations in written and oral form.

Throughout, you will use AI at high intensity and under tight control. The course’s defining message is simple:

AI is your arm and your research assistant — not your brain. AI may propose. The researcher must verify. The evidence must support.

Learning Outcomes

By the conclusion of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Find a gap and scope a problem. Locate a meaningful gap in a field you care about and turn it into a well-scoped, researchable problem.

  2. Ask answerable questions and classify them on the inquiry compass. Turn a problem into questions evidence can address, and place each on the compass by kind (descriptive vs causal) and reach (data at hand, a population beyond it, or cases not yet seen), justifying the classification and its limits without heavy mathematics.

  3. Declare a research design on the library pathways. Match a classified question to a design (observational or experimental, descriptive or causal, with prediction treated as its own objective) and declare it with the MIDA framework: model, inquiry, data strategy, answer strategy.

  4. Diagnose and redesign before collecting data. Simulate a declared design, read its diagnosis, and redesign it so it can actually answer the question.

  5. Gather and analyze evidence responsibly with AI. Direct AI tools to locate sources, operationalize concepts, and analyze evidence, and verify every result and citation against real, retrievable evidence while documenting each decision.

  6. Attack your own analysis. Stress-test a finding with robustness checks and adversarial red-teaming, and reproduce a peer’s package in a replication and red-team review.

  7. Communicate under uncertainty, in writing and out loud. Present a claim with its boundary, uncertainty, and limitations as a poster at the Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo, a written research note, and a public evidence defense.

  8. Manage AI as a directed research assistant. Run the Specify, Delegate, Interrogate, Inspect, Verify, Document, Defend workflow, coordinate multiple AI roles, and keep a complete AI Research Ledger, because AI is your arm and your research assistant, not your brain.

How the Course Runs

The course meets Monday, Wednesday, and Friday in person, in 50-minute sessions, and each day of the week has a fixed job.

  • Monday and Wednesday are flipped lectures led by a rotating Student Research Lead (SRL). From Week 2 on, one of you runs each lecture as a Socratic investigation, not a slide summary: pose a puzzle, get the room to commit to an answer before opening any AI tool, direct the AI investigation, and defend a decision at the end. The instructor monitors for accuracy and formalizes the concept. You will lead about five times across the semester, and your leading is graded.

  • Friday is a project studio. No new topic content is introduced on Fridays. Each studio runs the same loop: a short research stand-up, a milestone sprint kicked off from that week’s Brightspace brief and worked with AI support, a peer and AI red-team review, and a submit step where you update your dossier and AI Research Ledger. The milestone you have been building all week is submitted at the close.

  • Two meetings are asynchronous online modules: one in mid-October (Friday, October 2) and one during Thanksgiving week (Monday, November 23, the replication and red-team module). Each async module is a self-contained unit with its own graded artifact.

  • There is no class the day after the Expo (Wednesday, November 18); the Expo presentation on Tuesday, November 17 stands in its place.

The Student Research Lead

Leading a lecture is a graded performance and a rehearsal of exactly what the Expo and the final defense will ask of you. Slots are assigned by rotation across five seats, so each of you leads roughly five times. You receive your lead’s SRL page five days ahead and submit a preparation script two days ahead for instructor review, then run the room live against a published rubric. The full guidance, templates, and rubric are provided in the course’s Student Research Lead materials, and your dates and formats live on the Schedule page.

Course Materials

  • Primary text (free): Blair, Coppock & Humphreys (2023), Research Design in the Social Sciences: Declaration, Diagnosis, and Redesign (Princeton University Press) — read free online at book.declaredesign.org; no purchase required. Chapters are read conceptually, with no quantitative prerequisite, on the light cadence in the course schedule.

  • Course book (free): the course’s own open text, Evidence-Driven Research: How to Design, Analyze, Verify, and Defend Empirical Research, published on the course website. It walks through the same material at an honors-undergraduate reading level and links each chapter to its Colab lab.

  • Optional companion: Bergstrom & West, Calling Bullshit (public case studies at callingbullshit.org), used lightly to sharpen claim evaluation. Any additional readings are posted on Brightspace.

  • Computing (required): A laptop or desktop with internet access and a modern web browser. Course activities run in the browser through Google Colab, with no local installation. There are no purchases required for this course.

  • AI tools: The course teaches responsible, documented AI use (see the AI Policy). Approved tools:

    • Google Gemini is the primary research assistant, used in and alongside Colab.
    • Purdue GenAI Studio supplies custom reviewer roles that you are required to consult at four milestones (M5, M7, M9, M13).
    • Microsoft Copilot (Purdue-provided, with data protection) and other general-purpose AI tools are permitted with disclosure.

Assessments

There is no conventional midterm or final exam. The course assesses the research chain itself: a semester-long individual research project carried through sixteen milestones (M0–M15), presented publicly at the Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo (Tuesday, November 17 — required), and closed with a public evidence defense and a reproducible final research chapter and AI-management portfolio. Grading rewards correctness, transparency, reproducibility, question-design alignment, and responsible interpretation, never coding elegance (no computing background is assumed; all code is provided).

Weights are a proposal subject to final confirmation.

Revision policy: feedback returns within 3 days of each milestone; most milestones accept a revised version within 7 days of feedback for up to half the lost points (the revising is part of the craft being graded). Terminal artifacts have no revision window: the M10 final poster (Friday, November 6, 5:00 PM), the M15 final research chapter and AI-management portfolio (Friday, December 11), and the live Expo presentation (Tuesday, November 17, a graded component of M12). For these, the deadline governs.

Late policy: −10% per day, up to 3 days, then not accepted (documented emergencies handled individually per Purdue policy).

Grading scale: A ≥ 93, A− ≥ 90, B+ ≥ 87, B ≥ 83, B− ≥ 80, C+ ≥ 77, C ≥ 73, C− ≥ 70, D ≥ 60, F < 60 (no curve).

Poster and Conference Policy

Presenting at the Purdue Fall Undergraduate Research Expo (Tuesday, November 17) is a required, graded component of the course. Your poster is locked for printing on Friday, November 6 at 5:00 PM (M10) and cannot be revised after that time. At the Expo you present your own poster and evaluate at least three colleagues’ posters using the course criticism instrument. The Expo presentation is a live event: only a documented emergency qualifies for a make-up arrangement, handled individually per Honors College norms.

Reproducibility Policy

Every analysis ships as a clean-run Colab package that reproduces from a fresh runtime with the course seed (SEED = 464), so the same inputs yield the same outputs on any machine. At the replication and red-team milestone (M13), you reproduce a peer’s anonymized package and attest whether its headline result runs, then report where it is fragile. Your project closes as a reproducibility capsule (M14): the notebook, data, and documentation a stranger would need to rebuild your claim.

Grade Challenges

Students who wish to challenge a grade must do so within 3 calendar days of the grade’s release. An exception applies for the last week of class, when assignments must be challenged within 1 calendar day to ensure timely computation of final course grades. Grade challenges must be grounded in legitimate disputes over research-methods principles or grading accuracy. Challenges based on post-hoc legalistic arguments or subjective dissatisfaction will not be considered.

To challenge an assignment score:

  1. Review posted solutions / rubrics thoroughly.
  2. If you still believe an error has been made, email the instructor with:
    • Course name, section, and date/time
    • Your name and Student ID
    • Assignment number / title
    • Specific deduction being challenged
    • Reason for the challenge, with reference to the solutions or grading rubric

Grades will not be discussed in class — before, during, or after class. Please use office hours for questions related to course content or assignment clarification. After the challenge period, all grades are final and cannot be revised further for purposes of calculating final course grades.

Course Policies and Additional Details

AI Policy

This course explicitly teaches responsible, documented AI use. You are expected to use AI at high intensity, under tight control, and always as your arm and your research assistant, not your brain.

The discipline. Every use of AI in this course follows the full workflow Specify, Delegate, Interrogate, Inspect, Verify, Document, Defend (the everyday shorthand is Ask, Verify, Document). You specify the task precisely, delegate it to the tool, interrogate and inspect what comes back, verify it against real evidence, document what you decided, and defend the result as your own.

The AI Research Ledger. Every deliverable and every notebook session appends an entry to your AI Research Ledger, with eight fields: task delegated, tool used, prompt, output summary, decision, verification method, remaining concern, and responsible student. A missing ledger entry returns the submission.

Verify everything. AI tools routinely fabricate citations, sources, and facts. Every claim and every source you submit must be independently located and confirmed to exist and to say what you claim it says. Any errors or omissions from your use of an AI tool are your responsibility. Undisclosed or unverified AI output is an academic-integrity violation.

What is yours alone. Some decisions are never delegated to an AI tool. You, and only you, are responsible for:

  • selecting the research problem and question;
  • declaring the research design and target population;
  • choosing measurements and evaluating data quality;
  • ethical judgments;
  • deciding which claims the evidence justifies;
  • acknowledging uncertainty and limitations;
  • defending the project publicly.

Approved tools. Google Gemini is the primary in-notebook research assistant. Purdue GenAI Studio supplies reviewer roles you must consult at four milestones (M5, M7, M9, M13). Microsoft Copilot and other general-purpose tools are permitted with disclosure. You are not permitted to use AI tools during any closed assessment where its use is prohibited. Low-effort prompts produce low-quality output; refine your prompts using the course concepts.
