20  20. AI as Analytical Assistant

The research decision. Decide which parts of your own analysis to hand an AI tool as an analytical assistant, and how to verify every number it returns against your data before you believe any of it. You own which checks count and which flagged problems are real; the tool only does the labor you specify.

20.1 Why this decision matters

“I do not care that a model ran your robustness checks. I care which ones you chose, which numbers you re-derived yourself, and which flags you confirmed against the data. Show me that trail and I trust your judgment. Show me the model’s transcript and I trust neither.” — a reviewer for a human-computer interaction venue, reading a usability study’s results section

An AI tool can now write your analysis code, run a dozen robustness checks, and hand you a clean table in seconds. That speed is exactly why a loose habit is dangerous. Paste the table into your poster and you have signed your name to numbers you never checked. The reviewer above wants the short list of decisions that stayed yours. This chapter gives you the habit that survives her question.

20.2 The concept

An AI analytical assistant is an AI tool you hand a well-specified analytical task, never your judgment. Example: you tell it “recompute this time gap after dropping the three most experienced users” and it writes and runs that one check. The task is checkable. The verdict is not its job.

The unit you attack is your headline estimate, the single number that stands in for your whole finding. Example: “users of the new interface finished about 0.7 standard deviations faster.” Before you defend that number, you try to break it, and this is the labor you delegate.

Two attacks do most of the work. A robustness check re-runs the same finding under a different but equally defensible choice and asks whether the answer holds. Example: you reported a mean completion time, so you also compute the median and check the story survives. A placebo test runs your exact analysis where the effect cannot exist and confirms it returns near zero. Example: you shuffle the “new interface / old interface” labels at random and check the fake gap collapses to nothing.

Here is the division of labor. The assistant proposes checks, writes the code, and prints the numbers. You decide which checks count, and you verify every number before it reaches your claim. A tool that ran without an error has not proven its result is correct. You verify the number, not the paragraph about the number.

20.3 A worked example

You are studying whether a command palette (a searchable menu you open with one keystroke) helps users format a document faster than the old nested menus. Your headline estimate: palette users finished about 0.7 standard deviations faster. You use an analytical assistant to attack it.

Delegate. You ask the assistant to recompute the gap across a small grid: all participants versus dropping the three who had used a command palette before (the sample), mean versus median completion time (the measurement), and the raw gap versus one adjusted for each user’s baseline typing speed (the specification).

Inspect and verify. The assistant returns eight numbers. You do not trust them yet. You recompute two rows by hand with a second, simpler expression, and they match. The direction holds across all eight, and the magnitude ranges from about 0.5 to 0.9 standard deviations. Your honest headline is a direction plus a range, not one flattering number.

Placebo and red-team. You ask the assistant to shuffle the palette labels and re-run. The fake gap comes back near zero, so the machinery is not manufacturing your effect. Then you ask it, as a hostile reviewer, for the single worst flaw. It says with total confidence that experienced users drive the result. You already dropped them in the sample check and the gap held, so the data refute that flag. The flaw it missed is the one that matters: completion time is not user satisfaction, so your claim must stay about speed, not preference.

The assistant did the arithmetic. You decided which checks counted and which flags the data confirmed.

20.5 Recommended AI prompts

Commit your own expectation first, then delegate. Each prompt is a checkable job, not a request for a verdict.

Propose the checks you might have missed.

Here is my robustness grid for one estimate: I vary the sample (all users /
drop prior palette users), the measurement (mean / median time), and the
specification (raw gap / typing-speed-adjusted). Act as a hostile reviewer.
Name up to three additional, equally defensible checks I did NOT list, and
for each say which direction you expect it to move the estimate.

After running, verify: check each proposal against your four handles and confirm you can actually compute it in your data. Counters illusion of completeness (a tidy grid that still omits the one check that would break the claim).

Delegate the computation, keep the numbers checkable.

Recompute my time gap under each row of this grid and return a table:
row label, the gap, and the exact columns and filter used for that row.
Do not summarize; show one number and its code path per row.

After running, verify: recompute two rows by hand with a second expression and confirm they match the table. Counters plausible-but-wrong-method (a row whose label and code quietly disagree).

Red-team the surviving claim.

Here is my claim: "the palette is about 0.7 SD faster." Act as a hostile
reviewer of HCI studies. Name the single worst way this result could
mislead, and the exact data check that would confirm or kill it. Do not
rewrite my claim.

After running, verify: run the check it names, and treat the flaw as real only if your own output confirms it. Counters sycophantic agreement (praise that reviews your ego, not your evidence).

ImportantDo not delegate

Three calls never leave your hands. You decide which checks count for the claim you want to make, which flagged problems the data actually confirm, and the final claim you defend, with its boundary and its range. The assistant proposes and computes. The evidence decides, and you are the one who reads the evidence.

20.6 An AI failure case

You hand an assistant your grid and it returns a polished eight-row table, every row near 0.7, and declares the result “fully robust.” The run threw no error. Here is the trap: its “median” rows silently call the same mean function as the “mean” rows, so four of the eight numbers are duplicates wearing different labels. The table agrees with itself because it never varied the handle it claims to vary. This is the illusion of completeness, a thorough-looking output missing the one thing that matters, shading into confident fabrication, a number stated with certainty that never came from the code path it names.

You catch it by recomputing one “median” row by hand. The true median gap is 0.55, not 0.70. The grid overstated how much your choices agreed. A green check is not a correct result.

20.7 Verification laboratory

Verify this chapter’s key result, the surviving robustness of your headline gap, with alternative code from the verification guide: recompute the same gap a second way and compare. Filter each group by hand, subtract the two means yourself, and confirm you land on the assistant’s number. Then add a second, independent road with a falsification test, the placebo: shuffle the group labels, re-run, and confirm the fake gap sits at zero. Two roads to one answer is a verified result. Two roads to two answers means you measured the assistant’s code, not your effect.

20.8 Project transfer

This chapter feeds your M9 poster draft and research audit, the graded record of how hard you tried to break your own claim and what survived. Take your own M8 estimate, delegate its robustness grid and a placebo to an assistant, and verify every number before it enters the audit. For each AI use, record the task you delegated, the number the tool returned, the named check that earned your trust, and the one flag you caught the tool getting wrong. That verified trail, plus your surviving headline stated with its range, is what you walk a skeptic through at Friday’s studio.

20.9 Defend your decision

In four sentences, defend one analytical task you handed an assistant. Name the check you delegated and the one number you re-derived yourself before you believed it. State the bounded claim you can now make, with its boundary (what the check does not establish) and its range (how far the estimate moved across your defensible choices). Close with your uncertainty: the one flaw no check could settle, and the verification you would run next to close it.
