16  16. Hybrid and Complex Designs

The research decision. When your question forces you to stitch several measurements or stages into one answer, decide whether that combined design still behaves as a single aligned whole you can diagnose before you run it, instead of assuming that stacking rigorous-looking pieces automatically produces a rigorous result.

16.1 Why this decision matters

“Do not hand me the headline number. Hand me the error budget: every quantity you measured, how you measured it, and how each one’s uncertainty flowed into the final answer. A precise result built on one uncharacterized step is a precise wrong answer.” — an experimental physicist reviewing a lab report that claims to have measured a constant

A complex design fails in a quiet way. Each piece looks careful on its own, so the whole looks careful too. But an answer assembled from several measurements is only as trustworthy as its worst-characterized piece, and the assembly can amplify a small mistake into a large one. This chapter gives you the discipline to take such a design apart and diagnose the whole before you trust its number.

16.2 The concept

Every research design has four parts, and the course names them MIDA: the Model (your written picture of how the world could work), the Inquiry (the one exact quantity you want from it), the Data strategy (how the data come to exist), and the Answer strategy (how you turn that data into an estimate). The Colab lab builds all four from scratch. This chapter takes them somewhere harder.

A complex, or hybrid, design is a research design whose four MIDA parts have more than one moving piece: several measurements, stages, or sub-questions stitched into a single answer. Example: computing a physical constant from two separate measurements, each with its own instrument and its own error.

Two ideas decide whether such a design holds together. The first is alignment, meaning all four parts point at the same single quantity. Complexity breaks it in a fresh way: two carefully built sub-measurements can each be fine yet quietly target slightly different quantities that never combine into the one you named. The second is error propagation, meaning each measured input’s uncertainty flows into the final answer, sometimes amplified. Example: if your answer depends on the square of a measured time, a 1% error in that time becomes roughly a 2% error in the result.

Keep the lab’s two failure words close. Bias is a systematic tilt that more data does not shrink; variance is run-to-run wobble that more data does. Complexity lets a small systematic error hide behind an impressively small random one.

16.3 A worked example

You want the local acceleration due to gravity, call it g, from a swinging pendulum. Your model is the small-angle pendulum, whose period obeys T = 2π√(L/g) and rearranges to the estimand you will report: g = 4π²L / T². That single formula is a complex design in miniature. It stitches two sub-measurements into one answer: the length L you read off a ruler, and the period T you time with a stopwatch.

Watch the anatomy. Timing one swing is noisy, because your reaction time adds a random error to each start and stop. So you make a smart data-strategy move: time 50 swings and divide by 50. That averaging crushes the variance, and your period looks beautifully repeatable.

Here is the trap the lab trains you to see. Repetition does nothing for bias. Let the pendulum swing wide and the small-angle model behind T = 2π√(L/g) no longer holds, so the period you measure runs systematically longer than the formula assumes. Your data strategy and your model have drifted apart, and because T is squared, that drift is amplified straight into g. A value of g that reads 9.72 and never wobbles is not a correct g, only a confidently wrong one. The fix is a redesign of the data strategy, swinging at a small amplitude so the model holds again, not one more round of timing.

16.4 The Colab laboratory

This chapter’s lab is notebook nb03 — The anatomy of a research design: MIDA + declare → diagnose → redesign. Open it in Colab with the badge above. There you build a small simulated world, declare a design as something a computer can run, diagnose it by simulating it thousands of times to read its bias, variance, and power, then redesign the one part that most improves it. The lab runs that loop on a causal social question; this chapter shows the same anatomy holds when the design is complex.

16.5 Recommended AI prompts

Commit your own answer first, then delegate. Each prompt is a checkable job, not a request for a verdict.

Locate the real rule.

Act as a physics-lab assistant. For a simple pendulum timed to estimate g via
g = 4π²L/T², name two things and cite a real, retrievable source for each: (1) the
amplitude below which the small-angle approximation holds to about 1%, and (2) the
standard rule for propagating the uncertainty in L and T into g. Only state values
you are confident exist.

After running, verify: open the cited source and confirm the amplitude threshold and the propagation rule actually appear there. Counters confident fabrication (an invented threshold arrives as confidently as a real one).

Decompose the design into pieces you can check.

Here is my combined design: [paste your model, inquiry, and how you measure L and T].
Break it into sub-designs. For each sub-measurement, name which MIDA part it lives in,
the one assumption it depends on, and how its error enters the final g. Return a table.

After running, verify: set the table beside your own decomposition. Any sub-design or assumption it skipped is exactly the gap to chase. Counters illusion of completeness (a tidy table that omits the one uncharacterized step).

Red-team the combined result.

Act as a hostile metrologist. Here is my measured g and its stated uncertainty:
[paste]. Name the single systematic error that more repetitions can never fix and the
place my four MIDA parts fail to point at the same quantity. Do not fix it for me.

After running, verify: if it only praises your precision, push back and demand the one worst systematic error. Counters sycophantic agreement (praise that reviews your ego, not your error budget).

ImportantDo not delegate

Three calls stay yours. You decide what single quantity your inquiry names (which g, under what conditions), whether each sub-design actually targets that same quantity so the parts align, and whether to trust a combined number your diagnosis says a systematic error dominates. A tool can propagate the arithmetic, but it cannot decide that a precise number is a correct one. That judgment is the whole point of the chapter, and it is yours.

16.6 An AI failure case

You paste your L and T into an AI tool and ask for g. It returns, with total confidence, g = 9.79 ± 0.02 m/s². The number sits right next to the textbook 9.81, the interval is tiny, and the code ran without an error. It is wrong. The tool propagated only the random timing variance, the part you already crushed by timing 50 swings, and never modeled the systematic bias from your wide swing amplitude. That tight ± 0.02 describes wobble it could see and stays silent about the tilt it could not.

You catch it in two moves. First, check the assumption the number rests on: was the amplitude actually small enough for T = 2π√(L/g) to hold? Second, run a falsification move: redo the timing at a deliberately larger amplitude and watch the “precise” g drift systematically downward. A quantity that shifts well outside its own stated interval had an uncertainty that was never honest. Verify the number, not the tidy interval beside it.

16.7 Verification laboratory

Verify the chapter’s key result, that a complex design’s precision does not prove it is unbiased, with simulation from the verification guide. Declare the combined design in code with a known g and a planted large-angle bias, run it many times, and read the two numbers apart: variance shrinks as you add swings, while bias stays exactly where the amplitude put it. Then add a second, independent check, direct calculation: recompute g by hand from your L and T. Two roads to one answer is a verified result; two roads to two answers means the pipeline, not the pendulum, is what you measured.

16.8 Project transfer

This chapter deepens your M3 research charter and MIDA declaration, the three-minute declaration you present at Friday’s studio. If your own project’s design is complex, meaning it draws on more than one data source, runs a two-stage answer strategy, or blends a descriptive sub-question with a causal one, do not declare it as a single black box. Decompose it into its sub-designs, confirm each aligns to your single inquiry, and diagnose the whole rather than each piece alone. Bring that decomposition and one named redesign to the studio as the diagnosis-and-redesign part of your declaration.

16.9 Defend your decision

In four sentences, defend one complex design. Name the sub-designs it stitches together and the single inquiry all of them must align to. State the bounded claim the whole design licenses, and say whether one sub-measurement’s systematic error dominates the result. Close with your uncertainty: the sub-measurement whose bias you have not yet ruled out, and the one check you would run next to close it.
