# Human Responsibility Checklist

*The decisions that stay yours no matter how good the AI gets, the loop that keeps
you in charge, and the rule for stopping when a delegated task drifts into
territory that is yours alone. AI is your arm and your research assistant, not
your brain.*

---

## Yours alone

AI can help you gather, draft, compute, and critique. It cannot own a research
decision. The seven decisions below are the ones you make, defend, and are
accountable for. If an AI output *makes* one of these for you rather than
*informing* it, you have handed away the part of the work that is the course.

- ☐ **Selecting the research problem and question.** What is worth asking, and
  what you will actually ask, is your judgment about what matters.
- ☐ **Declaring the research design and target population.** The kind of inquiry,
  its reach, and the design that licenses it are yours to declare and defend.
- ☐ **Choosing measurements and evaluating data quality.** What counts as a good
  measure of your concept, and whether your data are trustworthy, is your call.
- ☐ **Ethical judgments.** Whether a study is fair to its subjects, honestly
  reported, and responsibly framed is a human responsibility, never delegated.
- ☐ **Deciding which claims the evidence justifies.** How far your findings reach,
  and where you stop, is the line only you can draw.
- ☐ **Acknowledging uncertainty and limitations.** Naming what you do not know and
  where your evidence is thin is an act of intellectual honesty you own.
- ☐ **Defending the project publicly.** At the poster, the Expo, and the evidence
  defense, you answer for the work. AI does not stand at the board.

Use this list as a literal checklist on every milestone: for each decision, could
you defend it as *yours*, or did an AI output quietly make it? If the latter,
take it back before you submit.

## The loop that keeps you in charge — SDIIVDD

The course trains one repeatable loop for working with AI. It puts a human
decision at the start and a human defense at the end, with verification in the
middle where trust is earned. In one page:

1. **Specify.** *You* decide the task and write the brief. You state your own
   expected answer first, before consulting AI, so you have something to compare
   against.
2. **Delegate.** You hand the well-specified task to the tool. Delegation is
   giving out *work*, never giving out *judgment*.
3. **Interrogate.** You push on the draft: assumptions, competing explanations,
   counterexamples, confidence. You argue with it like a reviewer.
4. **Inspect.** You look at what it actually produced, closely, not at the summary
   of what it says it produced.
5. **Verify.** You confirm the output is true with a named method from the
   [verification guide](verification_guide.md). No trust before this step.
6. **Document.** You record the task, prompt, decision, and verification in your
   [AI Research Ledger](ai_research_ledger_template.md).
7. **Defend.** You can explain and stand behind the result in your own words, to a
   skeptical human, without the tool in the room.

The everyday shorthand is **Ask → Verify → Document**. If you ever find yourself
going Ask → Use, you have skipped the two steps that make the work yours.

## The escalation rule

Here is the rule that connects the loop to the "yours alone" list:

> **When an AI output touches one of the seven decisions above, you stop
> delegating and decide.**

Delegation is fine right up to the boundary of a human-only decision. At that
boundary the tool's job is to inform you, and your job is to choose. The moment an
output would *settle* the research problem, the design, the measurement, the
ethics, the claim boundary, the uncertainty, or the defense, you take the pen
back. You may use everything the AI gathered; you may not let it make the call.

Two quick tests for whether you have crossed the line:

- **The defense test.** Could you defend this decision, in your own words, to a
  hostile reviewer, without saying "the AI said so"? If not, you have not yet
  made the decision; the tool has.
- **The accountability test.** If this decision turns out wrong, who answers for
  it? If the honest answer is "me" (and it always is), then the decision was
  always yours to make, not to accept.

When you escalate, log it: note in the ledger's decision column that the task
reached a yours-alone boundary and that you made the call, with your reasoning.
The full procedure is in the
[escalation protocol](escalation_protocol.md).
